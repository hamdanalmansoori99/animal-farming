## Behavioural and manning

<ProblemCard
  problem="Bird won't step up onto the glove"
  causes="Under-manned bird, fear association (something scared it recently on the glove), weight too high, or the glove smells of another bird or predator."
  action="Check weight first — a bird well above flying weight has no reason to step up. Offer food on the glove at the correct weight, in a quiet environment, without eye contact. If the issue is fear, go back to basic manning for a week or two: short calm sessions, gentle presence, no pressure."
/>

<ProblemCard
  problem="Mantling food aggressively when you approach"
  causes="Weight is too low and the bird is defending a precious meal, or the bird has learned that people take food away. Common in newly trapped birds or those previously handled roughly."
  action="Do not remove food from a mantling bird — it reinforces the aggression. Let the bird finish the meal, then check weight. Raise weight slightly for several days to reduce food-guarding. Feed calmly on the glove at consistent times."
/>

<ProblemCard
  problem="Constantly bating on the perch"
  causes="Poor manning, under-exercised bird, weight too low for the bird's mood, noisy or stressful environment, too close to other birds, or the perch position faces something frightening."
  action="Check perch placement — move the bird away from high-traffic areas and line of sight to other birds. Reassess weight. Extend manning sessions daily for a week. Review Housing — the daily room may be too small or too busy."
  vet="If bating is sudden and new in a previously settled bird, rule out pain (injured leg, foot, or wing) before assuming behavioural cause."
/>

<ProblemCard
  problem="Screaming (especially imprinted birds)"
  causes="Imprint-related attention-seeking — the bird learned as a chick that vocalising brings food or contact. Often permanent in hand-raised birds; harder to undo once established."
  action="Never feed or interact with the bird while it is screaming. Wait for a moment of quiet, then reward with food or calm approach. Consistency is everything — one weak moment teaches the bird to scream longer next time. Prevention in chicks is easier than correction in adults."
/>

<ProblemCard
  problem="Refuses to tolerate the hood"
  causes="Poor early hood training, the hood fits badly, a painful association (hood pulled on roughly), or eye sensitivity."
  action="Check hood fit first — too tight, too loose, or the wrong shape cannot be fixed with more training. With a correctly-fitting hood, go back to hood-acclimation basics: brief hood-on, immediate reward, gradual duration increase. Never struggle with the hood — that teaches resistance."
/>

## Training

<ProblemCard
  problem="Won't fly to the lure from distance"
  causes="Weight too high, under-trained at short distances before progressing, the bird can't see the lure clearly, or the training environment is too distracting."
  action="Drop back to short-distance lure work until consistent response, then extend gradually. Check weight — a full bird has no motivation to work. Train in a quiet environment before introducing distractions. Make sure the lure is visually appropriate for the species."
/>

<ProblemCard
  problem="Won't return after being flown"
  causes="Weight slightly too high, distracting environment, nearby wild prey, or the bird has not yet been flown free enough to generalise recall. For trained birds, sometimes a simple bad moment — prey flushed, weather shifted."
  action="Recall the bird calmly to the lure from the ground. If no response within a reasonable window, break out telemetry. For persistent issue, drop flying weight slightly and retrain recall on creance before flying free again."
  vet="Not a vet issue — but a lost bird in Gulf summer can dehydrate fast. Telemetry is non-negotiable."
/>

<ProblemCard
  problem="Takes off after unwanted targets"
  causes="High wild-prey density, bird is above flying weight and confident enough to chase, or under-trained on specific quarry."
  action="Fly at a time and place with fewer distractions. Reassess weight. For persistent chasing of specific non-target species, use telemetry and carry a strong lure for rapid recall."
/>

<ProblemCard
  problem="Reluctant to step onto the scale or perch"
  causes="Unfamiliar scale surface, bad prior experience, hesitancy from handling change, or sore feet that make the landing surface unpleasant."
  action="Use the same scale daily — a bird that knows the scale will step up without hesitation. Offer a small food reward on the scale for a few days to build positive association. Inspect feet for any tenderness."
  vet="If reluctance is new and the bird favours one foot, check for bumblefoot or injury — see the Health chapter."
/>

## Feeding and condition

<ProblemCard
  problem="Refusing food today"
  causes="Weight too high, illness, stressful event earlier in the day, spoiled or unfamiliar food, or the bird still has casting to pass from the previous meal."
  action="Check casting first — never feed until the previous casting is out. Verify food is fresh and at the right temperature (not still partly frozen). If weight is normal and food is fresh, observe for any other signs. Skipping one meal in an otherwise well bird is rarely urgent; two is worth investigating."
  vet="If the bird has refused food for 48 hours or shows any other sign (droppings changes, lethargy, open-mouth breathing), call the vet — see the Health chapter symptom triage."
/>

<ProblemCard
  problem="Won't come down to flying weight"
  causes="Feeding portion too large, food too rich (too much day-old chick fat, for example), recent increase in rations, moult-era appetite, or the bird is metabolically slow."
  action="Trim the meal by small amounts over several days — never crash the weight. Shift diet toward leaner prey (whole quail, trimmed chicks). Verify the scale is accurate. Be patient — slow, steady drops are healthier than fast ones."
  vet="If weight will not shift despite reduced rations and the bird appears otherwise normal, have a vet rule out fluid retention or organ disease."
/>

<ProblemCard
  problem="Sudden weight drop without explanation"
  causes="Illness (top of the list), parasites, sour crop, environmental stress (heat, new housing), or a missed meal you didn't log."
  action="Do the 60-second health check from the Health chapter immediately — stance, eyes, cere, breathing, crop, casting, droppings, feet, plumage. Check the log for a missed meal. Weigh again at the same time tomorrow before drawing conclusions."
  vet="A drop of more than 5–10% from flying weight without clear cause is a vet call — same-day if possible."
/>

<ProblemCard
  problem="Bolting food or regurgitating shortly after a meal"
  causes="Weight too low (desperate eating), prey item too large to swallow comfortably, food too cold, or early sour crop."
  action="Present smaller prey pieces. Warm frozen-thawed prey to room temperature before feeding (not hot, not still cold). Raise weight slightly. Observe the crop empties normally after the next meal."
  vet="If regurgitation is foul-smelling, contains slime, or is repeated across multiple meals, that is sour crop until proven otherwise — vet today. See the Health chapter."
/>

## Minor physical

<ProblemCard
  problem="Broken blood feather (bleeding pin feather)"
  causes="The bird knocked a growing feather — usually during the moult, against a perch, cage bar, or in a bate. The feather shaft is still vascular and will bleed until the feather is pulled or the shaft seals."
  action="Restrain the bird safely. With pliers and a firm steady pull in the direction of feather growth, pull the damaged feather out at the base. Apply styptic powder or a ferric chloride pencil to the feather follicle if bleeding continues. Keep the bird calm for a few hours afterward."
  vet="If the feather breaks off without pulling cleanly, or if bleeding persists after standard stopping measures, take the bird to a vet — retained shaft fragments cause infection."
/>

<ProblemCard
  problem="Small cut or abrasion on foot"
  causes="Caught a perch splinter, bated into something sharp, or a minor scratch from handling equipment."
  action="Clean the wound with sterile saline. Inspect under good light for any foreign material. Apply a thin layer of clean antiseptic (vet-approved; no human products with strong chemicals). Keep the bird on padded perches until healed. Monitor for heat or swelling daily."
  vet="Any wound that does not begin to heal within a few days, shows heat, swelling, or discharge, or is deeper than surface — call the vet. Infected foot wounds become bumblefoot quickly."
/>

<ProblemCard
  problem="Overgrown beak — needs coping"
  causes="Insufficient natural wear, usually because the bird doesn't get whole-prey bone to work on, or an individual with a faster-growing beak."
  action="Coping is a precise vet procedure — the beak has live tissue and vasculature, and cutting into the wrong area causes pain and bleeding. Check the Nutrition chapter and increase whole-prey with bone content as prevention."
  vet="Coping itself should be done by a vet or a highly experienced falconer. Do not attempt with household tools — it is easy to cut too far and hit the quick."
/>

<ProblemCard
  problem="Overgrown talons"
  causes="Insufficient wear from weathering on natural surfaces, Astroturf perches exclusively without abrasive alternatives, or individual growth rate."
  action="Adding a single rougher-surface perch (stone or textured wood) to the rotation often solves it naturally. If trimming is needed, it is a similar precision job to coping — the quick is visible under strong light and must not be cut."
  vet="Have the vet do the first trim so you can see exactly how much to take off. Subsequent light maintenance can be done at home once you know your bird's anatomy."
/>

## Equipment

<ProblemCard
  problem="Hood doesn't fit properly"
  causes="Wrong size for the species and individual, stretched leather, or a hood made to the wrong head shape. Signs: bird constantly works the hood off, breathes heavily when hooded, or the hood sits crooked."
  action="Do not try to train around a badly-fitting hood. Source a new hood from a maker who works to your species — saker, peregrine, and gyrfalcon hoods are different shapes and sizes. A well-made, well-fitting hood is transformative."
/>

<ProblemCard
  problem="Jesses fraying fast"
  causes="Wrong leather (too soft), jesses too long so the bird treads on them, rough perch edges chafing, or the bird has unusually abrasive behaviour."
  action="Inspect daily — see the Equipment Care section in Daily Care. Replace at the first sign of significant wear; a broken jess in flight can mean a lost bird. Source jesses made from the right leather weight for your species. Verify perch edges are smooth."
/>

## Environmental

<ProblemCard
  problem="Open-mouth panting on the perch"
  causes="Heat stress — the bird is trying to cool itself. In Gulf summer this is common and can escalate to heat stroke. More sensitive in gyrfalcons, less so in sakers."
  action="Move the bird immediately to shade and fresh airflow. Offer a bath or fine mist. Cool the bird's environment — AC if indoors, shade structures if outdoors. Review the Daily Care weathering rules: no midday weathering in Gulf summer."
  vet="If panting continues after cooling, or the bird becomes weak or disoriented, treat as heat-stroke emergency — vet immediately."
/>

<ProblemCard
  problem="Won't bathe or avoiding the bath bowl"
  causes="Water too cold, too deep, or the bowl has a slippery surface. Sometimes seasonal — birds bathe more willingly in warm weather. Can also signal illness (a sick bird avoids bathing)."
  action="Offer room-temperature water in a wide, shallow, non-slip bowl. Place it where the bird is comfortable — some birds prefer bathing outside the daily room. Try again every day or two. Most healthy birds bathe readily when the right conditions are offered."
  vet="A bird that was bathing happily and suddenly stops, combined with any other sign, warrants a vet check. Avoidance of bathing alone is usually preference or conditions, not illness."
/>

## Tendencies by species

The problems above apply to all three falcons. What differs is **which problems are most common for each species**. Switch the species tabs above.

<SpeciesOnly species="saker">
### Saker — most common problems

The saker is the **most straightforward of the three** on the problem front. Most issues that do arise are training-motivation related — the saker is forgiving enough that mild routine breaks don't spiral, but its adaptable temperament means it can also lose focus when under-exercised.

**What you'll see most often:**
- **Training motivation dips** — especially mid-season; more a diet/weight conversation than a behavioural one
- **Mild bumblefoot warnings** from hot summer perches (see Health)
- **Food preference drift** — the saker's varied diet tolerance can turn into fussiness over weeks of repetition

**Less common:** stress-driven behavioural problems, heat-panting (the saker handles Gulf heat better than the other two).
</SpeciesOnly>

<SpeciesOnly species="peregrine">
### Peregrine — most common problems

The peregrine's problem profile is **dominated by stress and handling sensitivity**. Issues that a saker shrugs off — a new person in the mews, a louder-than-usual training session, a single day off routine — can show up as behavioural problems in a peregrine.

**What you'll see most often:**
- **Bating and nervousness** in response to environment changes
- **Hood resistance** if early hood training wasn't patient
- **Handling stress** during weighing, equipment changes, or transport
- **Feeding behaviour** is sensitive — peregrines bolt food when stressed

**Less common:** weight management struggles (small body + bird-specialist diet makes precise weight work well), heat-panting (peregrines tolerate moderate heat better than gyrs).
</SpeciesOnly>

<SpeciesOnly species="gyrfalcon">
### Gyrfalcon — most common problems

The gyrfalcon's problem profile is **dominated by the Gulf summer**. Outside summer it's a steady, capable working falcon; inside summer almost every issue ties back to heat sensitivity.

**What you'll see most often:**
- **Open-mouth panting** at ambient temperatures that don't bother sakers or peregrines
- **Weight-management struggles in summer** — appetite drops, flying weight shifts, training response falls off
- **Reduced motivation in heat** — training sessions that work in March become untenable by July
- **Bathing behaviour shifts** — gyrs bathe more in summer because they need the cooling

**Less common:** frequent behavioural stress issues (gyrs are relatively even-tempered), food-guarding (their size makes them confident feeders).

**Most important reframe:** when a gyr develops a problem in summer, heat is the first thing to check — before diet, before training, before health.
</SpeciesOnly>
