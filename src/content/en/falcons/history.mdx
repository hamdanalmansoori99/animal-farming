The falcon on your glove is a living link to roughly **four thousand years** of continuous practice. The same three species that Mongol khans trained, that Bedouin tribes captured in passage, that Frederick II of Hohenstaufen catalogued in the 13th century, that Cornell scientists brought back from the edge of DDT extinction in the 20th — are the species you weigh, feed, and fly today.

This chapter is the story of that tradition, organised chronologically from ancient origins to the modern Gulf. It is not filler. The ten technical chapters of this guide — nutrition, training, health, breeding, and the rest — sit inside the historical and cultural frame this chapter describes. The craft is worth doing well precisely because of what it has been, and because of what it still is.

## Ancient origins — four thousand years on the steppes

The origin question is not fully settled. The earliest archaeological evidence of falconry in Central Asia dates to the **first millennium BC**; rock etchings in the **Altai Mountains** (circa 1000 BC) show hunters with birds of prey. Some historians push origins back further still — theorised practice as early as **2000–4000 BC** in the Mongolian steppe — though evidence for those deeper dates is indirect.

The ancient Mesopotamian record adds a second strand. The *Epic of Gilgamesh*, the world's oldest surviving substantial literary work, contains references to hunting with birds of prey that historians read as falconry-adjacent practice in the Iraq of roughly **2000 BC**. Persia and the Iranian plateau appear in the early record too. The **"parallel evolution" hypothesis** — falconry arising independently in the Mongolian steppes and in Persia at roughly the same time, then spreading outward — reconciles the two traditions without forcing a single point of origin.

What is not in doubt is that by the time the Silk Road trade routes were fully established, falconry had already been practised across Central and Western Asia for many centuries. Every later tradition — Arab, Chinese, European — emerged from, or converged on, this ancient core.

## The Silk Road and the spread

From the Central Asian heartland, falconry moved along the corridors of trade, migration, and conquest.

Westward, it reached Persia, the Levant, and the Arabian Peninsula. Eastward, it reached China and the Korean peninsula; by the **Tang Dynasty (7th–10th c. AD)** it was an established imperial sport in China. Southward, it reached the Indian subcontinent. The Mongol empire, at its 13th-century peak, moved falconry along the entire length of Eurasia with exceptional refinement — the Great Khans practised it both for food and for sport between military campaigns.

By the time of the **Abbasid Caliphate (8th–13th c. AD)**, falconry was firmly established as a sport of Arab rulers and is referenced in the Arabic literary record of the period. UNESCO's Silk Roads Programme recognises falconry as one of the defining **living heritages** of the trade-route world — a craft that travelled along the routes alongside silk, spices, and ideas, and that maintained its cultural continuity wherever it took root.

## Medieval Europe — Frederick II and the royal sport

Falconry arrived in Europe by multiple routes — earlier than commonly supposed — but it was the **Crusades** of the 11th–13th centuries that brought the polished Arab-Persian tradition into direct contact with European courts. The result, in the next generation, was the most important falconry text ever written.

**Frederick II of Hohenstaufen** (1194–1250) — Holy Roman Emperor, king of Sicily, crusader, and one of the most scientifically curious rulers of the Middle Ages — commissioned and authored ***De Arte Venandi cum Avibus*** ("On the Art of Hunting with Birds"). He spent **roughly thirty years compiling the work** and completed the text in the 1240s, with annotations by his son Manfred.

The **illuminated Vatican manuscript** — Manfred's version, still held by the Vatican Library — contains **over 900 bird illustrations** and is widely recognised as **the first zoological treatise written in the critical spirit of modern science**. Frederick's insistence on **direct observation** over received authority — his willingness to contradict Aristotle when his own observations disagreed — set a standard that would not be matched in European natural history for centuries.

Medieval European falconry beyond Frederick became a **marker of rank**. The hawk-of-station tables of the late medieval and early modern period (formalised most famously in the 15th-century *Boke of Saint Albans*) assigned specific birds to specific social ranks — gyrfalcon for the king, peregrine for a prince or earl, saker for a knight, lanner for a squire, and down the hierarchy. The tables were more literary than operational, but they reflect how seriously European aristocracy took the association between falcon and rank.

## The Arab and Bedouin tradition

The Arab falconry tradition **far predates** the modern Gulf states. References in **pre-Islamic Arabic poetry** and in early Islamic-era texts describe hunting with trained falcons, and the Arabic language developed over centuries a **specialised lexicon** for the craft — ages, colour phases, conditions, and techniques — that the language of no other tradition matches for depth.

At the heart of the Arab tradition is the **Bedouin seasonal cycle**. Desert tribes captured **migrating passage falcons** — historically sakers and shaheens passing through on autumn migration — and flew them through the winter on the quarry the desert afforded: **houbara bustard, stone curlew, hare**. When spring came and the birds would have migrated back north, they were **released**. The falcon was understood as a **partner across a season**, not a permanent possession. The partnership was renewed each year with a new bird.

The vocabulary still carries this tradition: **الحرّ** (*al-hurr*, the saker) literally means "the free one"; **الشاهين** (*al-shaheen*, the peregrine) carries pre-Islamic poetic resonance; **الجير** (*al-jeer*, the gyrfalcon) the northern visitor. The falconer is **الصقّار** (*al-saqqar*). The specialised desert tradition spread across the Arabian Peninsula, Iraq, Iran, the Levant, the Maghreb, and into Central Asia, with regional variations in vocabulary and technique.

![Warm desert dawn over open terrain — the landscape and light in which the Bedouin falconry tradition was practised for centuries before it evolved into the modern Gulf industry](/images/dawn-landscape.png)

This seasonal, partnership-based model is the direct cultural ancestor of the modern Gulf falconry industry. Some of its rhythms — the pre-flying conditioning, the weight-keeping, the release of certain birds back to the wild, the moult discipline — survive in modern practice even where the industrial scale has changed everything else.

## The Gulf story — tribal practice to national heritage

The late-20th-century transformation of Gulf falconry is one of the most dramatic cultural continuities in any craft. As oil wealth arrived, **falconry moved from practical hunting to cultural statement** — and then back to practical craft, but at a scale and technical sophistication no previous generation could have managed.

**Sheikh Zayed bin Sultan Al Nahyan** (1918–2004), founding president of the United Arab Emirates, was one of the most consequential figures in that transformation. His personal devotion to falconry — and his national-scale commitment to it — drove the founding of dedicated breeding programmes, the **Abu Dhabi Falcon Hospital** (established 1999, now one of the largest falcon-specific veterinary facilities in the world), the **Dubai Falcon Hospital**, the **Souq Waqif Falcon Hospital** in Doha, and the international-diplomacy dimension that eventually produced the UNESCO inscription. The UAE's commitment was matched by parallel programmes in Qatar, Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, Bahrain, and Oman.

The modern Gulf infrastructure layered onto the ancient tradition includes **captive-bred certification**, **microchipping**, **the falcon passport** (for international movement), **CITES documentation**, and a network of specialised veterinary care that far exceeds what existed anywhere before. The craft is now practised at **industrial scale** alongside — not replacing — the hobbyist and hereditary tiers, and the Gulf is the global centre of gravity for the entire world's falconry industry.

See the **Breeding** chapter for how the Gulf captive-breeding programmes work in technical detail, and **Breeds & Types** for the hybrid crosses (gyr × saker in particular) that are the Gulf's most distinctive contribution to modern falconry.

## The 20th century crisis and recovery — DDT and the copulation hat

The mid-20th-century **peregrine collapse** is the single most consequential event in modern falconry history. Widespread agricultural and public-health use of the pesticide **DDT** from the 1940s onwards caused catastrophic **eggshell thinning** and reproductive failure in peregrine falcons and other raptors across North America and parts of Europe. By the 1960s the North American peregrine had effectively **disappeared from much of its historical range**. Other species — bald eagle, osprey, brown pelican — were also hit.

The recovery effort was led by a coalition of **conservationists, ornithologists, and falconers**. The **US banned DDT in 1972**. The **Peregrine Fund**, founded in **1970 at Cornell University**, became the institutional heart of the peregrine recovery programme. The falconry community contributed both funding and — crucially — the technical knowledge of captive husbandry and breeding that formal academic ornithology at the time did not yet have.

The decisive technical breakthrough came from **falconer Lester Boyd** of Pullman, Washington, who invented the **copulation-hat artificial insemination technique**. The first successful hatch from the method was in **1975**. Boyd's innovation, adopted into Cade & Weaver's *Falcon Propagation* manual and refined in the decades since, is the direct technical foundation on which **every large-scale modern captive-breeding programme** — including the commercial and hybrid programmes of today's Gulf — is built.

By the **1990s the peregrine had recovered sufficiently** in North America to be removed from the US endangered species list (**delisted in 1999**). European populations recovered similarly. The recovery is recognised as one of the great conservation successes of the 20th century, and the alignment between **falconry and conservation** that it produced is permanent: modern falconry cannot be separated from the conservation institutions that saved the bird it depends on.

## UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity

In **2010**, falconry was inscribed on the UNESCO **Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity**. The inscription was **led by the United Arab Emirates** and co-nominated by ten other countries: **Belgium, Czech Republic, France, South Korea, Mongolia, Morocco, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, Spain, and Syria**.

The list has been **extended multiple times**:
- **2012** — Austria, Hungary
- **2016** — Germany, Italy, Kazakhstan, Pakistan, Portugal
- **2021** — Croatia, Ireland, Kyrgyzstan, the Netherlands, Poland, Slovakia

The current total stands at **24 countries** — making falconry **the largest multi-national element on the UNESCO Representative List**, and one of the most geographically continuous living traditions recognised by UNESCO anywhere.

What the inscription means practically is that the participating national governments have formal international obligations to **support and safeguard the living tradition** — museums, training programmes, documentation efforts, regulatory protection. For the falconer on the ground, the inscription is both a recognition of what the craft is and a promise that it will continue to be supported.

## Falconry today

Falconry is not a museum piece. It is a **living craft that is actively evolving**.

- **The Gulf is the modern epicentre.** More serious falconry capital, more captive-bred production, more specialised veterinary infrastructure exists in the Gulf than in the rest of the world combined. The UAE, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, and Kuwait host the most developed programmes.
- **Competitive falconry** — falcon **racing** (the President's Cup in Abu Dhabi is the flagship), beauty events, hunting competitions — draws international participation and substantial prize money.
- **Conservation infrastructure** — the **Houbara Fund** and comparable programmes maintain the wild prey base that working falconry needs, connecting the modern sport back to the ecological reality the Bedouin tradition depended on.
- **International associations** — the **International Association for Falconry and Conservation of Birds of Prey (IAF)**, the **North American Falconers Association (NAFA)**, **The Falconers Club** in the UK, and regional Gulf associations coordinate across borders and lobby for legal and conservation protections.
- **The technical knowledge base is still expanding.** Veterinary medicine, breeding techniques, telemetry, nutrition science, and training methodology all continue to develop. The technical chapters of this guide exist because the practice is a live, evolving discipline, not a recitation of fixed lore.

## Why this matters for the falconer on the glove

You have just read four thousand years of history. The bird on your glove is:

- The same species pre-Islamic Arab poets wrote about
- The same species Frederick II watched, catalogued, and flew
- The same species that came back from the edge of extinction after DDT
- A living thread back to the Altai rock-etchings of 1000 BC

The responsibility that implies — to keep the bird well, to honour the lineage, to pass the knowledge — is what gives the ten technical chapters of this guide their weight. Get the nutrition right, get the housing right, watch for the symptoms that tell you something is wrong. Not because any single action is heroic but because the cumulative craft is four thousand years old and the bird in your care is carrying that forward.

### Saker — the historical working bird

The **saker** is the working bird of the **Mongolian steppes** and the **Bedouin desert** — the species at the heart of the classical Arab falconry tradition and of the Central Asian tradition that preceded it. The Arabic name **الحرّ** ("the free one") captures the traditional Bedouin relationship with the bird: caught in passage, flown through a winter, released in spring.

**Historical role:**
- The **Central Asian and Arab working falcon** across millennia
- Flown on ground-dwelling quarry — houbara, stone curlew, hare — because sakers in the wild are generalist hunters that take ground mammals as readily as birds
- The species least involved in the European royal tradition (sakers appeared in European falconry but were never central there as they were in the Arab world)
- In the modern Gulf: still the **majority species** of serious commercial production, the dam side of most gyr × saker hybrid crosses, and the species on which the bulk of the Gulf's breeding and veterinary infrastructure is optimised

The saker is the falcon the Arab tradition was built around. Every modern practice — from weight-keeping to moult discipline — traces in part to the Bedouin relationship with this species.

### Peregrine — the European royal and the DDT survivor

The **peregrine** is the species most woven into **European falconry** — Frederick II's own subject, the falcon of medieval kings and princes in the hawk-of-station tables — and, in the 20th century, the **icon of modern conservation**. Its near-extinction under DDT and its recovery through captive breeding reshaped the relationship between falconry and conservation worldwide.

**Historical role:**
- The **European royal bird** — prince or earl in the hawk-of-station tables, Frederick II's extensively-catalogued subject, the classical falcon of western falconry
- The Arabic name **الشاهين** (*al-shaheen*) carries pre-Islamic poetic resonance and predates the modern taxonomy; the bird was well known across the Arab world as a migrant visitor in passage
- The **central species of the DDT crisis and recovery** — the falcon that The Peregrine Fund was founded to save (1970), the species Lester Boyd's copulation-hat method first succeeded with (1975), and the species whose US ESA delisting in 1999 marked the completion of one of the great conservation successes of the 20th century
- In the modern Gulf: important in its own right, and critically important as the tiercel side of the historical copulation-hat innovation that now underpins all commercial captive breeding

To fly a peregrine today is to fly a bird whose modern existence in captivity is itself a conservation success.
</SpeciesOnly>

<SpeciesOnly species="gyrfalcon">
### Gyrfalcon — the bird of kings

The **gyrfalcon** is historically the **rarest, largest, and most prized** of all falcons. In medieval European hawk-of-station tables, the gyr was reserved for **the king alone** — no lesser rank was entitled to carry one. The **white-morph gyrfalcon**, in particular, was a **diplomatic gift between sovereigns** — part of the exchange between the European courts, the Mongol khans, and the Arab rulers of the high medieval period.

**Historical role:**
- The **bird of kings** — Nordic, Mongol, and medieval European; reserved for the highest rank in every tradition that encountered it
- The **white-morph gyr** as diplomatic currency — sent between rulers as a gift of the highest symbolic weight
- Historically associated with the **Arctic**, where the bird breeds; in Arabic known as **الجير** (*al-jeer*), the northern visitor
- In the 20th century: also affected by DDT, though less catastrophically than the peregrine, and extensively captive-bred through the same programmes that saved the peregrine
- In the modern Gulf: the **flagship prestige bird** and — critically — the **tiercel side of the gyr × saker hybrid**, the Gulf's defining modern cross. White-morph gyrs remain the single most culturally and commercially valuable birds in the Gulf market, preserving the "bird of kings" status that is older than the modern states themselves

To keep a gyrfalcon in the Gulf is to continue a specific historical line — the tradition of the most prized falcon being kept at the highest level of care, as it has been for a thousand years.
</SpeciesOnly>
