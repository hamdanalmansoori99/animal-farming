Training a falcon is a **relationship across weeks and months**, not an event. Three principles carry the whole arc:

- **Trust** — built through manning, reinforced through consistency, destroyed by rushing.
- **Weight** — the language the bird and falconer share. Correct weight makes training work; wrong weight guarantees failure.
- **Consistency** — same time, same place, same sequence, every session.

Hold those three and the falcon teaches itself half the rest. Break any of them and no technique will fix what follows.

For manning fundamentals, see **Daily Care**. For flying weight mechanics, see **Nutrition**. For red-flag symptoms that mean pause-and-call-the-vet during training, see **Health & Prevention**. For when training stalls or the bird misbehaves, see **Common Problems**. This chapter is the training arc itself.

## The training arc — phases in order

A bird moves through these phases in sequence. Each phase is measured in days or weeks, not hours. Rushing any one of them sets back the next two. The phases are:

### 1. Manning
The calm, patient acclimation of the bird to you and to your environment. Full coverage in Daily Care. For training purposes, the transition from "manned" to "training" happens when the bird stands calmly on the glove, ignores normal household sound, tolerates hooding, and accepts food from your hand without fear or aggression. Rushing out of manning into active training is the most common reason training stalls later.

### 2. Hood training
The hood is not just an accessory — it is the **transition mechanic** for all future training. A calm hood-on marks the move from active mode to rest; hood-off marks the move from rest to work. A bird that accepts the hood easily has a clear mental switch between training and not-training. Practise smooth hood-on / hood-off daily during manning and continue through all future training.

### 3. Step-up to food on the glove
The foundation action. The bird steps from a perch or from the scale onto the gloved fist for food. Starts at near-zero distance (the glove right in front of the perch), progresses as the bird becomes confident. This is usually the first "action" under active training and should be solid before anything else begins.

### 4. Short hops to the glove
The first "flight" in training. The bird, from a near perch, hops a few centimetres across to the glove for food. Gradually increase distance — perch to glove, across a small gap, across a metre, across two metres. The bird is still tethered (jesses held). This phase builds the recall association between glove and reward.

### 5. Creance work
Controlled flights on a long lightweight line. Full detail in the dedicated section below. This is where short hops graduate into real flights, but still under full control — the creance catches any mistake. A typical training arc spends two to six weeks on the creance before considering free flight.

### 6. Free flying transition
The decision to release the creance is the single biggest judgement call in early training. Too early and the bird may not return; too late and you miss the natural progression. Indicators of readiness: consistent recall from full creance length, good weight control, good health, and — critically — a working telemetry setup tested on the ground.

### 7. Lure training
The primary recall and conditioning tool for flown falcons. Full detail in its own section below. Lure training starts once free flight is established and continues as a lifelong conditioning tool.

### 8. Entering to quarry
The bird makes the leap from lure-as-reward to live prey. The transition from training exercises to actual hunting. Some falconers combine this with lure training; most treat it as a separate late phase. Full detail below.

## Weight management during training

![A saker falcon on a perch scale during the daily weigh-in](/images/nutrition/weighing.png)

Weight management during training is where the abstract concept from the Nutrition chapter becomes the **daily practical lever**. A bird one gram too heavy won't come; a bird one gram too light won't fly well. The falconer learns the bird's precise working range over weeks of observation.

- **Setting a starting flying weight.** Individual to each bird and variable by species. Sakers, peregrines, and gyrfalcons all have different typical ranges (see Nutrition); within species, individuals vary. The starting flying weight is the falconer's best initial estimate, adjusted daily.
- **Daily weigh-in rhythm.** Same scale, same time, before food. Log every reading. The trend matters more than any single number.
- **Adjusting weight based on response.** If the bird is unresponsive or distracted, it's often slightly above weight. If the bird is frantic, food-guarding, or bolting food, it's often slightly below. The right weight produces calm, keen, confident flying.
- **Never force weight down fast.** A drop of more than 5–10% from the bird's normal flying weight without clear cause is a red flag for illness (see Health). A gradual, controlled weight-down over a week or two is the right rhythm, not two days of heavy reduction.
- **Seasonal weight shifts.** Flying weight is not static. Summer heat raises metabolic needs; moult recovery shifts it again; winter cold tightens it. Expect adjustments across the year.

A reliable flying weight, tracked on a trusted scale, kept in a clear log — that is half of training.

## Creance work — in detail

The creance is a long lightweight training line (typically 15–30 metres), attached to the falcon's jesses via a swivel, with the other end secured to a safe anchor or held by the falconer. It allows you to practise controlled flights at progressively increasing distance without risking a lost bird.

### Setup

- **Open, level ground** with no obstructions that the line could snag on
- **Calm weather** — strong wind or visible wild prey will distract a bird in early training
- **Known safe anchor** or a capable assistant holding the line firmly
- **Tested swivel and line** — a fraying creance is a bird-safety failure
- **Correct weight for the bird** — see the weight-management section

### Progression

- **First sessions:** calls at 2–3 metres, bird flies directly from a near perch or post to the glove for food. Several short successful calls per session, not one long session.
- **Gradual distance increase** over days and weeks. A well-progressed bird will reach full creance length (15–30 m) consistently before graduating to free flight.
- **Read the bird.** Confident, direct flight with strong wing-stroke = ready for more distance. Hesitant, slow, or multi-stop flight = stay at the current distance or step back.
- **Do not extend distance in a session.** Extend between sessions. Ending on success at a comfortable distance teaches the bird the pattern is reliable.

### Signs of readiness to transition off creance

- Consistent clean recall from full creance length for multiple consecutive sessions
- Strong flight posture — no dragging wings, no hesitation
- Good weight control — the bird is keen at the right weight, not desperate
- General health at baseline — not moulting, not recovering from illness
- Working telemetry fitted and tested before the first free flight

## Lure training

![A saker falcon in controlled descent toward a traditional leather lure mid-swing](/images/training/lure.png)

The lure is a leather pad with feathers or streamers tied to a cord, which the falconer swings to simulate prey motion. It is the primary recall and conditioning tool for flown falcons, and arguably the single most used piece of training equipment across a falcon's working life.

### What the lure does

- **Conditions recall** — the bird learns that the lure on the ground or in motion means food and a known safe landing
- **Builds fitness** — swung and retreating lure work makes the bird chase, stoop, and pursue, which conditions flying muscle
- **Maintains training** — a falcon that is not regularly flown loses edge; a weekly lure session keeps the response sharp

### How it is presented

- **Swung through the air in smooth arcs** that imitate prey motion, then allowed to fall so the bird can reach it
- **Pulled away at the last moment** for conditioning work, teaching the bird to stoop harder and pursue
- **Allowed to be taken** as reward on a schedule — the bird that never catches the lure loses motivation

### Schools of training

Two broad approaches exist:

- **Lure as primary recall** — the bird learns to return to the lure from any distance, the foundational recall behaviour
- **Lure as conditioning** — beyond recall, the lure becomes a daily fitness and responsiveness tool, keeping the bird tuned between hunts

Most working falconers use both — the same lure, different day-to-day purposes.

### Mistakes to avoid

- **Letting the bird foot (catch) the lure every time** — removes the motivation to stoop harder
- **Rewarding mid-stoop** — teaches the bird to break off from pursuit for the reward
- **Only practising at one distance** — the bird doesn't generalise recall
- **Presenting the lure without motion** — reduces the pursuit-and-chase reinforcement

<SpeciesOnly species="peregrine">
**Peregrines** often respond dramatically to the lure — they are natural high-pitch birds and will rise, stoop, and deliver hard strikes that look spectacular. Reward generously for good stoops; the peregrine's response to the lure is one of the defining experiences of training this species.
</SpeciesOnly>

<SpeciesOnly species="gyrfalcon">
**Gyrfalcons** bring raw power to lure work but tire faster in heat than sakers or peregrines. In Gulf summer, keep lure sessions short and cool; the gyr will give everything in each stoop but needs recovery time between.
</SpeciesOnly>

<SpeciesOnly species="saker">
**Sakers** are steady, reliable lure workers — less dramatic than a peregrine or gyr, but extremely consistent. A well-trained saker will return to the lure across large distances in varied conditions with predictable response, which is exactly what working falconry needs.
</SpeciesOnly>

## Stooping and pitch training

For peregrines and gyrfalcons particularly, a mature training programme includes **height-seeking** behaviour — teaching the bird to climb high above the falconer and "wait on" at altitude, ready to stoop on game that is flushed below.

- **A stoop** is the high-speed diving attack from altitude — the classic falcon image, and the peregrine's natural hunting mode.
- **Pitch** is the height at which the bird waits on above the falconer or the anticipated quarry. Higher pitch = more dramatic stoop, more momentum, more successful kill on strong prey.
- **How height is encouraged:** delayed lure presentation (bird circles above waiting for the falconer to produce the lure), ground-based quarry that the bird has to descend to reach, consistent timing of rewards.
- **When this is appropriate:** advanced training, not early. A bird that has not yet mastered recall, creance, and basic lure work is not ready for pitch training. Rushing height work produces low, flat, unproductive flights.
- **For sakers:** pitch training is less central. Sakers are generalist hunters in the wild, taking game from the ground and from flushed low flight; they don't naturally seek the same pitch as peregrines or gyrs. Most working saker training emphasises direct pursuit rather than classical stooping from altitude.

## Entering to quarry

The transition from training exercises to actual hunting — the moment the bird makes the mental leap from "lure means reward" to "live prey is the reward". This is a bigger shift than it sounds; some birds make it instantly, some need several attempts, a few never fully commit.

- **Why this step matters.** Until a bird has successfully taken live quarry, all training has been a simulation. The first successful flight at real game rewrites the bird's understanding of why it is flying at all.
- **How to do it safely.** Controlled setup — a known, reasonable quarry for the species; open terrain; no other birds in the area; telemetry on; the falconer clear-headed and unhurried.
- **Common first-quarry species by region.** Varies widely. In the Gulf, traditional quarry includes houbara bustard (heavily regulated — follow all local laws and conservation rules), stone curlew, and smaller game. Outside the Gulf, grouse, pigeon, rabbit, duck are common. Match the bird to quarry it can reasonably take — a first kill should be achievable, not heroic.
- **What to do when it goes right.** Let the bird feed fully on the kill the first few times — the reward has to be unmistakable. Don't rush to retrieve the bird.
- **What to do when it goes wrong.** The bird misses, becomes discouraged, or refuses to engage. Back up a step — more lure work, more creance, more recall practice — before trying again.

<SpeciesOnly species="saker">
**Sakers** are often the easiest to enter to quarry, especially on ground-dwelling game. Their generalist wild diet means the mental leap is natural; they take to hunting readily with good training.
</SpeciesOnly>

<SpeciesOnly species="peregrine">
**Peregrines** are bird-specialist hunters and typically excel at flighted game — ducks, pigeons, pheasants. Their entry to quarry often happens at high pitch, dramatic stoops, and clean hard strikes. Less natural on ground-dwelling quarry.
</SpeciesOnly>

<SpeciesOnly species="gyrfalcon">
**Gyrfalcons** bring the most raw power to first-quarry work — but in the Gulf, seasonal timing matters most. Heat-sensitive by nature, the gyr's best entering-to-quarry window is the cooler months. Summer entering rarely goes well.
</SpeciesOnly>

## Telemetry and lost-bird recovery

![Falconry telemetry equipment laid out — transmitter, receiver, directional antenna](/images/training/telemetry.png)

**Every falconer flying a bird free must understand telemetry before the first free flight.** This is not optional. A bird without telemetry is a bird you are betting you will not lose — and you will eventually lose that bet.

### The three components

- **Transmitter** — a small unit attached to the bird (usually on a leg or tail-mount), emits a radio signal continuously
- **Receiver** — handheld unit in the falconer's hand that detects the signal strength and direction
- **Directional antenna** — typically a Yagi-style antenna with prongs, held in the hand, rotated to find the strongest signal direction

Together they let you walk down a signal until you find a bird that is out of sight.

### Pre-flight checks

- **Battery fresh** on transmitter and receiver
- **Signal tested** from at least a few hundred metres before first flight of the day
- **Direction bearing** established — know where the signal is when the bird is in sight, so you can compare when it's out of sight
- **Backup battery** in the falconer's bag

### What to do when a bird is lost

- **Stay calm.** A panicking falconer misses signals.
- **Begin the telemetry walk-down immediately.** The signal is strongest directly toward the bird; rotate the antenna slowly, follow the direction of strongest signal, walk in that direction, repeat every few hundred metres.
- **Call for help if you can.** A second pair of eyes and ears triangulates faster than one.
- **The first 24 hours.** Most recovered birds are recovered in the first 24 hours. A bird still hunting or roosting nearby is a recoverable bird. A bird that has flown too far to hear is far harder to recover.
- **Gulf-specific notes.** Heat is the biggest threat to a lost bird in Gulf summer — a falcon left a day in the sun can dehydrate fatally. Wind direction affects drift (a bird carried downwind is found downwind). Known water sources (ponds, oases, farm irrigation) are where a distressed bird often ends up — check them early.

## Season progression

The training rhythm shifts across the year — training is not a single continuous effort but a cycle that matches the bird's natural calendar.

- **Pre-season conditioning** — coming out of the moult, re-manning (birds lose some tolerance during the rest period), rebuilding fitness with short creance and lure sessions. Feels like starting over but progresses faster than the first time.
- **Mid-season** — full training cycle, hunting, daily rhythm locked in. This is when the bird is at its peak, typically autumn through early spring in the Gulf.
- **End-of-season** — transition toward rest. Weight gradually relaxed upward, training frequency reduced, prepare the bird for pre-moult condition.
- **Rest and pre-moult** — training stops. The bird moves to the dedicated moulting chamber (**المقيض**) where daily handling drops to a minimum and full rations are restored. See **Housing & Environment** for chamber design and moult management.

## Common training pitfalls

A short reference of the most frequent mistakes. For a deeper troubleshooting view with specific issues, see **Common Problems**.

- **Rushing the phases.** Moving to creance before step-up is reliable, or to free flight before creance is consistent, is the most common cause of training failures.
- **Training while the bird is unwell.** Any sign of illness pauses training. See the Health chapter symptom checks.
- **Training at the wrong weight.** Too heavy and the bird is indifferent; too light and the bird is desperate. Both ruin sessions.
- **Training in distracting environments.** Other birds, wild prey, strong wind — all undercut the training signal. Choose training grounds deliberately.
- **Inconsistent reward delivery.** Sometimes rewarding from the fist, sometimes from the lure, sometimes not at all — teaches the bird that the pattern isn't reliable. Be consistent.
- **Flying free without telemetry.** The one training pitfall that ends careers instead of delaying them. Never do it.

## Tendencies by species

### Saker — training notes

The saker is the **classroom falcon** — the species where training mistakes are most often recoverable, and the species most tolerant of an imperfect routine. A saker kept on a rough but consistent schedule produces a reliable working bird; a saker kept on a strict precise schedule produces an exceptional working bird.

**How the saker trains:**
- **Slower to peak performance** than a peregrine or gyr — the saker takes its time learning, which feels frustrating in weeks 2–4 and feels like gold by week 8
- **Very reliable once trained** — the saker's retention is excellent; what is learned stays learned across seasons
- **Forgiving of inconsistency** — a missed day, a slightly-off weight, a different handler occasionally doesn't derail a well-trained saker
- **Best suited to generalist hunting** — ground-dwelling quarry, mixed terrain, varied quarry types

**Training mistakes that rarely break a saker:** a single rushed lesson, a slightly heavy weight on one day, occasional schedule variance. Mistakes that still do break a saker: flying without telemetry, training while ill, gross weight mismanagement over weeks.
</SpeciesOnly>

<SpeciesOnly species="peregrine">
### Peregrine — training notes

The peregrine is **the fastest to progress and the most demanding of precision**. When training is correct, a peregrine advances through phases at a pace that can feel almost alarming. When training is imprecise, a peregrine regresses visibly within days.

**How the peregrine trains:**
- **Fast progression when done right** — creance work that takes 4 weeks with a saker may take 2 with a peregrine
- **Punishes inconsistency with visible regression** — miss a day, change the routine, and the next session shows it
- **Most rewarding experience** — the peregrine's high-pitch lure work and clean stoops are what draws many falconers to the sport
- **Best suited to flighted quarry** — ducks, pigeons, pheasants; the bird-specialist natural hunting mode translates directly

**Training mistakes that hurt a peregrine:** inconsistent reward schedule, handling pressure during stress, training environments with too much visual stimulation. Get those right and the peregrine rewards the effort with exceptional flying.
</SpeciesOnly>

<SpeciesOnly species="gyrfalcon">
### Gyrfalcon — training notes

The gyrfalcon's training calendar is **dominated by the Gulf summer**. Outside of summer it is arguably the most impressive of the three species in full flight; inside summer, training becomes progressively impossible as heat rises.

**How the gyrfalcon trains:**
- **Spring and winter are gold** — cool weather training with a gyr produces flights that are the signature experience of classical falconry
- **Summer training is not "train carefully" but "do not train"** — past a certain heat threshold the bird shuts down responsiveness
- **Best suited to high-pitch lure and stoop work** — the gyr's cold-climate origin gives it the musculature and endurance for dramatic altitude and long stoops
- **Raw power over precision** — where a peregrine is surgical, a gyr is overwhelming. Train for that.

**The defining tendency:** when you plan a training season for a gyrfalcon, plan the calendar around heat first, training goals second. A gyr kept training through Gulf peak summer is a gyr at elevated risk of aspergillosis, heat stress, and long-term condition loss. The chapter on Housing & Environment covers the seasonal transitions in detail.
</SpeciesOnly>
