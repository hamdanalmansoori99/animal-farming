Breeding is the hardest thing a falconer does. It is the part of the craft most likely to fail, most likely to produce heartbreak, and most likely to require calling a vet in an emergency that cannot wait until morning. It is also the part of the craft with the heaviest regulatory burden — CITES, national breeding licenses, microchipping, ringing, captive-bred certification — and the single largest investment of facility, time, and money.

None of that is a reason to avoid it. But it is a reason to enter it with open eyes.

This chapter is organised in the rough order of the breeding year: **purpose → legality → pair selection → choosing a method → facility → photoperiod → courtship → laying → incubation → hatching → chick rearing → fledging → hybrids → breeding-specific health → post-season → records → common mistakes**. Cross-references run back into **Health & Prevention** (egg-bound emergency, chick aspergillosis), **Nutrition** (laying-female calcium demand), **Supplements & Vitamins** (calcium gluconate, moult-recovery compounds), **Housing & Environment** (chamber design), **Daily Care** (post-fledge handling), and **Breeds & Types** (which hybrids are worth producing).

The numbers in this chapter — incubation temperatures, humidity ranges, hand-feeding intervals, clutch sizes, species-specific durations — are pulled from the Peregrine Fund's *Falcon Propagation* manual (Cade & Weaver) and the Peregrine Fund species pages. Where numbers are narrow safe ranges, the chapter says so.

## Why breed — clarify purpose before starting

Before any protocol, before pair selection, before facility investment, the keeper has to decide **why** they are breeding. Four purposes dominate, and each drives completely different decisions downstream:

- **Hobby or legacy line.** A small number of bonded pairs, producing a few chicks a year, mostly for the keeper's own flying or for close friends. Low volume, moderate facility, documentation matters but pressure is low.
- **Commercial production for sale.** Multiple pairs, often using artificial insemination to maximise productive tiercels, producing chicks for the Gulf and international falconry market. High facility investment, strict documentation, serious regulatory exposure.
- **Hybrid production.** A subset of commercial, focused on producing gyr × saker or other intentional crosses. Artificial insemination is effectively mandatory; imprint tiercels are the core investment.
- **Conservation or research under permit.** Rare, permit-only, typically run by institutions or conservation-affiliated breeders. Not the focus of this chapter.

Pick one before reading further. A facility, method, and documentation set that fits a hobby line is not what a commercial hybrid operation needs, and vice versa. The rest of this chapter is written to cover all four, but the reader self-selects into the relevant sections.

## Legal and regulatory — the gate before anything else

No chick legally changes hands without documentation. Skipping this section and investing in infrastructure first is one of the most expensive mistakes a new breeder makes.

- **CITES** — the Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species. Gyrfalcons and peregrines are **CITES Appendix I** in many jurisdictions (the strictest tier); sakers are commonly **Appendix II**. Any international movement of a falcon requires CITES paperwork, and captive-bred birds need the corresponding captive-bred certificate (often referred to by article number in national implementations).
- **National breeding licenses.** UAE, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, Bahrain, and Oman each run specific falconry and breeding licensing regimes administered by their environment and wildlife authorities. The UK has its own, the US has its own federal + state system. You need the national license **before** you begin producing chicks for sale.
- **Microchipping and ringing.** Mandatory in most jurisdictions for captive-bred certification. Rings are fitted while the chick's tarsus is still small enough to accept the closed ring — typically **day 10–14** for falcons. Microchips are injected under the skin, typically from **day 21 onwards** once the chick is large enough. Both numbers are recorded in the bird's permanent file.
- **Veterinary certificates.** Captive-bred certification from an avian vet, plus health certificates for transport — both are commonly required for any legitimate transfer of a chick to a new owner.
- **Gulf-specific.** The UAE's *Ministry of Climate Change and Environment* licenses and regulates falcon breeders; Abu Dhabi and Dubai each have programmes with detailed standards. Qatar, Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, Bahrain, and Oman have analogous authorities. Registered falcon hospitals (Dubai Falcon Hospital, Abu Dhabi Falcon Hospital, Souq Waqif Falcon Hospital) play a documentation role in many Gulf programmes.

This chapter is reference, not legal advice. Before building anything, contact the relevant authority in your jurisdiction and an established IAF-affiliated breeder for the current procedure — rules change and vary country to country.

## Pair selection

The most consequential decision of the whole process. A bad pair and a perfect facility produce nothing; a good pair and a merely adequate facility still produce birds.

- **Age of the adults.** Falcons reach sexual maturity at **2–3 years**; peak productivity **3–8 years**; production declines after **10–12 years** in most lines. A "young proven" pair (3–5 years old, already produced a fertile clutch) is usually the best available purchase.
- **Proven vs unproven pairs.** A **proven pair** has produced at least one fertile clutch of chicks. An **unproven pair** has not. Proven pairs command substantial premiums for good reason — many pairings simply never produce, for reasons that aren't always diagnosable.
- **Condition.** Full body condition, clean recent moult, no known serious clinical history. Calcium status of the female is especially important — a female in poor condition entering the breeding season is at elevated risk of egg-binding and soft-shell eggs.
- **Genetic considerations.** Avoid close inbreeding. For hybrid crosses, document the lineage of each parent. Most reputable breeding operations maintain multi-generation pedigrees.
- **Compatibility.** Some pairs simply do not bond. Introduction is done slowly, with visual contact first, then shared airspace, then shared chamber. Pairs that show aggression rather than courtship after several weeks should be separated and re-paired rather than forced.
- **Imprint vs chamber-raised adults.** This is the decision that branches the whole method. **Chamber-raised adults** (parent-raised from the nest, minimal human contact) are the input to **natural pair breeding**. **Imprint tiercels** (hand-raised from within the first week after hatching) are the input to **artificial insemination with the copulation hat**. You cannot interchange them — an imprint tiercel will not pair-bond with a female, and a chamber-raised tiercel will not donate semen on the hat.

## Method 1 — Natural pair breeding

Most traditional of the three methods. Fewest tools required. Highest dependence on bird temperament and pair compatibility.

- **Setup.** A dedicated breeding chamber — purpose-built, visually isolated from other birds, with a clear nesting ledge and one or two calm perches. Details in the facility section below.
- **Introduction.** The pair is introduced in the off-season, with the tiercel typically placed in the chamber first and the female introduced days later. Early days are observed for signs of aggression vs courtship.
- **Courtship behaviours to watch for.** Mutual food-passing (tiercel presents food to the female), bowing displays at the ledge, shared ledge visits, soft vocalisations between the pair, and eventually the female soliciting the male's presence at the ledge.
- **Copulation.** In a well-bonded pair, copulation happens repeatedly over weeks — often dozens or more times leading up to laying — on or near the nesting ledge. The falconer observes via camera; you do not need to see it live to confirm it is happening.
- **Advantages.** Parent-raised chicks become **behaviourally normal falcons** — no imprint on humans, full species-recognition, best possible temperament for working or flying birds. Natural pair breeding is the method of choice for producing birds you plan to train and fly.
- **Limitations.** Not every pair bonds; the rate varies by lines and individuals. Cross-species pairing is effectively impossible by this method — you cannot natural-pair a gyr tiercel to a saker female. Success depends on temperament, experience of the birds, and facility quality.

## Method 2 — Artificial insemination with the copulation hat

The backbone of modern commercial and hybrid production worldwide, including essentially all large-scale Gulf hybrid operations.

**The history matters** because it explains the technique. The copulation-hat method was invented in the 1970s by falconer **Lester Boyd** of Pullman, Washington, during the peregrine recovery effort that followed the DDT population collapse. The first successful hatch from copulation-hat AI was in **1975**. The technique was adopted into **Cade & Weaver's *Falcon Propagation* manual**, published by the Peregrine Fund, and has been the standard reference for captive falcon breeding worldwide ever since.

### The imprint tiercel — a multi-year commitment

The male that donates semen for this method is an **imprint tiercel** — a bird taken from the nest within the **first week after hatching** and hand-raised in constant, intensive human contact for months. By the time it reaches sexual maturity at 2+ years old, the bird has bonded on its human handler and sees the handler as its mate.

- **It is a specialist bird.** An imprinted tiercel is useful only for AI — it cannot be trained for working falconry, cannot be released, and cannot be re-socialised back onto another falcon.
- **The investment is substantial.** Hand-rearing, multi-month intensive handling, a dedicated housing arrangement, and 2+ years before the bird is breeding-age — all before any semen is collected.
- **The tiercel's lifespan of productivity is long.** A well-kept imprint tiercel may donate reliably for ten-plus years, so the up-front investment amortises across many clutches.

### The copulation hat

The hat itself is a **rubber or silicone "turban"** worn on the handler's head (or sometimes on the fist or forearm). Its upper surface is textured with **small dimples** that collect the semen when the tiercel copulates on it.

- **Design.** The shape is species-appropriate — sized to the tiercel's footing and stride.
- **Material.** Smooth, hygienic, non-reactive; sterilised between sessions.
- **Use.** The handler presents themselves to the tiercel during the breeding season, the tiercel courts and mounts, and the semen collects in the hat's dimples. A trained tiercel does this reliably, often several times per session.

### Semen collection, evaluation, and storage

- **Collection timing.** During the courtship season, daily sessions are possible; peak collection from any individual tiercel is **roughly every 2–3 days**. Sessions last minutes.
- **Evaluation.** A fresh sample is checked under microscope for **concentration and motility** before use. A poor sample is discarded — using marginal semen wastes an insemination window.
- **Storage.**
  - **Fresh** — viable at room temperature for about **2 hours**.
  - **Chilled** (held at roughly **4 °C**) — viable for up to **24 hours**.
  - **Frozen** — used occasionally in research and advanced programmes; rare in routine commercial production. Cryopreservation of falcon semen is an active research area.

### Insemination of the female

- **Who does it.** A trained technician — AI on falcons is not a casual learn-from-a-video skill. Most commercial Gulf breeding operations have dedicated AI staff.
- **How.** Cloacal (vaginal) insemination with a **fine-tip syringe**, placing a small measured dose into the female's cloaca during her receptive window. The female must be handled calmly — most operations prefer to train the female to accept brief hooded restraint during the pre-breeding season so that AI sessions are low-stress.
- **Timing relative to lay.** Successful AI typically happens **3–5 days before the first egg**, and is continued through the laying window to maintain fertility across the clutch. Multiple insemination sessions across the receptive period increase fertility rates.
- **Frequency.** Several inseminations per female across the pre-lay and laying window is typical in commercial practice.

### Advantages and limitations

- **Advantages.** Allows hybrid production. Allows one proven tiercel to sire multiple clutches across multiple females. Avoids pair-bonding failures that kill natural-pair attempts. Permits a breeder to build a stable of known-fertile tiercels whose offspring is genetically traceable.
- **Limitations.** The imprint tiercel is a multi-year specialist investment. The AI technique has a learning curve and errors produce failed clutches. The female must be trained to accept calm handling. Hand-reared offspring grow more slowly than parent-reared and take longer to reach each developmental milestone — an honest expectation to set before committing.

## Method 3 — Cooperative or mixed methods

In practice many Gulf operations combine elements of both. Common patterns:

- **Chamber pair + supplemental AI.** A bonded natural pair housed in a chamber, with AI used to boost fertility mid-season or to introduce semen from a specific unrelated tiercel for genetic diversity.
- **Surgical AI.** A veterinary technique — semen deposited directly into the oviduct under anaesthesia — used in specific programmes where cloacal AI has been unreliable. Not common in routine production; a specialist technique.
- **Fostered clutches.** Eggs from one pair hatched under another pair, or eggs pulled for artificial incubation to induce a second clutch ("double-clutching") from the original female. Common in commercial practice.

These mixed methods all borrow the facility, documentation, and husbandry principles of the two primary methods above. This section is a brief overview, not a full protocol — each cooperative approach has its own specifics that a practitioner would work out with an experienced mentor and an avian vet.

## Breeding facility — chamber design

Purpose-built breeding chambers are not daily rooms with a new sign on the door. They are a different space for a different purpose, and facility investment here is substantial and permanent.

- **Dimensions.** Larger than a daily room — the pair needs space to display, to share a ledge, and to fly short distances comfortably. Working minimums for a single-pair chamber are roughly **3 × 3 m floor, 2.5–3 m ceiling**; bigger is better for peregrines and gyrfalcons especially.
- **Privacy.** Visual isolation from other birds is essential. Minimal human traffic, no line of sight to distractions (children, dogs, other falcons), solid walls rather than bars on external sides. A breeding pair that can see activity outside its chamber rarely settles into productive courtship.
- **Nesting ledge.** A specific structure — typically a shallow tray at shoulder height with a **soft substrate** (fine sand or pea gravel) deep enough for the female to make a shallow scrape. Positioned for **indirect natural light**, never in direct sun, and shielded from wind or drafts. Size should match the species: gyrfalcon ledges are larger than saker ledges.
- **Perching.** One or two calm padded perches at chamber height. Not stacked — the pair needs stable choice points, not a jungle gym.
- **Climate control.** The principles from **Housing & Environment** apply, but stricter. Birds cannot easily be moved during laying and incubation, so the chamber's climate must hold throughout the breeding window without intervention. AC and humidity control run continuously.
- **Camera or discreet observation.** Essential. A small fixed camera positioned to cover the ledge and main perching area, viewed remotely by the falconer, makes the difference between knowing what's happening and guessing. Disturbance-free observation is not a luxury — it is how you know when the female has laid, when she is sitting, and when a problem is developing.
- **Bath bowl and water.** As in the daily room — a wide, shallow, cleanable bath bowl refreshed regularly, plus fresh drinking water. Bathing supports laying-female condition.
- **Waste management.** Daily cleaning outside the chamber as much as possible; never during peak courtship or laying. Many breeders use quick-remove pull-out waste trays so cleaning happens in seconds from outside.

See **Housing & Environment** for the broader principles that carry over. Breeding adds these requirements *on top of* daily housing, not instead of them.

## Breeding season and photoperiod

Falcon breeding is **photoperiod-driven** — day length is the physiological signal that triggers sexual activity. This matters because Gulf breeders manage it differently from breeders in the natural Northern-hemisphere seasons the species evolved with.

- **Natural season.** In the temperate Northern hemisphere, falcons breed **February through May** — courtship in late winter, egg-laying in March–April, chicks through May–June.
- **Gulf-specific.** Gulf breeding runs **slightly earlier** — courtship and laying typically **late January through April**, hatching in February–May, avoiding the peak summer heat during the chick-rearing window.
- **Photoperiod manipulation.** Advanced commercial operations use **artificial lighting** to shift the breeding window earlier or later than the natural cycle — extending day length from roughly December onwards to bring pairs into breeding condition before natural photoperiod would. This is a specialist technique with real risks to the birds if done incorrectly; a new breeder should not attempt it without mentorship from an experienced commercial operation.
- **Multiple clutches per season ("double-clutching").** By pulling the first clutch of eggs for artificial incubation, some operations induce the female to lay a second clutch within the same season. Standard in commercial Gulf production, less appropriate for hobby or legacy-line breeding.

## Courtship and laying

Once the pair is in breeding condition, behaviour escalates in a recognisable sequence. The falconer watches via camera.

- **Early courtship.** Mutual food-passing (the tiercel brings food to the female), bowing displays, ledge visits with soft vocalisations. Weeks of this before laying.
- **Pre-lay signs.** The female spends **increasing time on the nesting ledge** 2–3 days before the first egg. Restlessness, adjustments of the scrape, and a noticeable shift in feed response (appetite often rises) indicate imminent laying. Calcium demand peaks here.
- **First egg.** The female lays on the ledge, typically in the morning. The tiercel may or may not visit the ledge immediately.
- **Clutch timing.** Subsequent eggs are laid at intervals of **48–72 hours** apart. A typical clutch is laid over **6–10 days** depending on species and individual.
- **Clutch size by species.**
  - **Saker** — 3–5 eggs is typical, with a documented range of 2–6
  - **Peregrine** — around 4 eggs is typical, with a documented range of 3–5
  - **Gyrfalcon** — 2–4 eggs is typical, with a documented range of 1–5
- **Egg appearance.** All three species lay roughly **buff or creamy-white eggs with fine chestnut or reddish-brown speckling**; colour patterns vary by individual female. Gyrfalcon eggs are the largest of the three, sakers intermediate, peregrines smallest.
- **Laying problems.** The breeding-specific health section below covers **egg-binding** (life-threatening), **soft-shell eggs** (calcium-related), and **cloacal prolapse** (vet emergency).

## Incubation

The most numerically precise phase of the whole year. Narrow safe ranges; errors cost embryos.

### Natural incubation — under the female

The female incubates the clutch with minimal tiercel participation in most pairs (some tiercels do briefly relieve the female; others never do). Key points:

- **When incubation starts.** Often after the penultimate or final egg is laid, so that all eggs develop roughly in sync and hatch within the same 24–36 hour window.
- **Female behaviour.** The sitting female leaves the ledge only briefly each day — for a bath, a drink, a short stretch — and returns quickly. A sitting female that repeatedly leaves the clutch for extended periods is a warning sign.
- **Tiercel role.** Provisioning — bringing food to the female so she does not need to hunt. In captivity the falconer provisions both.
- **Advantages.** Lowest-intervention method. Produces parent-raised chicks (see Chick Rearing below). Less equipment cost, less risk of operator error.

### Artificial incubator — when and why

Commercial operations routinely use incubators because:
- AI-produced eggs must be incubated artificially (no natural pair is sitting)
- A first clutch can be "pulled" (removed for artificial incubation) to induce the female to lay a second clutch — "double-clutching" — doubling the season's production
- Eggs from an unreliable sitter can be rescued and finished in the incubator
- Specific research or conservation programmes require controlled incubation

### Incubator parameters — narrow safe ranges

These numbers are the canonical Peregrine Fund values used across professional falcon breeding. They are narrow safe ranges; drift outside them kills embryos.

- **Temperature.**
  - **Forced-air incubators** — **37.5 °C (99.5 °F)**, held steady with well under a degree of drift either way.
  - **Still-air incubators** — run slightly higher at the shell top (roughly **37.8–38.3 °C / 100–101 °F**) because the air stratifies inside. Forced-air is the commercial standard.
- **Humidity.**
  - **Days 1 to (full term − 3)** — **30–45 %** relative humidity. Too high and the egg does not lose enough water during development; too low and the membrane dries. Falcon eggs, coming from generally dry climates, are on the drier end of avian norms.
  - **Final 3 days ("lockdown")** — **rise to 65–70 %**. The embryo is about to pip; humidity rises to soften the membrane and prevent the chick drying out during hatching.
- **Turning.**
  - **Minimum 3 times daily** during the main incubation window; ideally **every 1–2 hours** in automatic incubators that turn continuously through a shallow arc.
  - **Stop turning at lockdown** (the final 3 days) — the chick is positioning for hatching and should not be disturbed.
- **Incubation duration by species.**
  - **Saker** — **30–32 days**
  - **Peregrine** — **33–35 days** (range reported in the literature: 30–36)
  - **Gyrfalcon** — **about 35 days** (range 33–36)
- **Candling** — use a strong focused light to backlight the egg and observe embryo development. Standard candling is at **7, 14, and 21 days**. At day 7 a fertile egg shows a clear developing blood vessel network ("the spider"); infertile eggs are removed. By day 14 the embryo is a visible mass; by day 21 the embryo fills most of the egg leaving an air cell at the top.
- **Air cell growth.** The air cell at the blunt end of the egg grows steadily through incubation as water evaporates through the shell. Under-humid incubation produces an oversized air cell; over-humid incubation produces a too-small one. Regular candling and egg-weight tracking let the incubator operator correct humidity if the air cell is not growing correctly.
- **Hatching window.** If all eggs were laid close together and incubation began on the same day, the full clutch hatches within a **24–36 hour window**.

### Assisted hatching

Rarely needed; usually harmful when done too early. A chick that has made an external pip but has not progressed after **24 hours of stable pip without movement** may benefit from carefully moistening the membrane around the pip hole and very slowly helping the chick unzip. This is a specialist intervention — an experienced breeder or avian vet only. Helping too early kills the chick by releasing it from the shell before its yolk has absorbed.

## Hatching and the first 48 hours

- **External pip.** The chick's egg tooth produces a small crack in the shell — the first visible sign of hatching, typically **18–24 hours before full emergence**. The chick is breathing the outside air but has not yet unzipped.
- **Unzipping.** The chick rotates inside the shell and chips a spiral line around the blunt end, eventually pushing the cap off and emerging wet, exhausted, and with a visible yolk sac absorbing. This takes hours and should not be interrupted.
- **First 24 hours.** The chick is drying, fluffing into down, and absorbing the remaining yolk sac. **It does not need food for the first 24 hours** — the yolk sac carries it. Chicks disturbed or force-fed in this window commonly die from aspiration or shock.
- **Second 24 hours.** The chick begins raising its head and gaping for food. First feedings are very small pieces of fresh lean meat (day-old chick breast is the standard), warmed to body temperature, presented on forceps. See the hand-raising feeding schedule below.
- **Placement.**
  - **Parent-raising** — returned to the nest ledge (or left there from hatching). The parent falcons take over immediately.
  - **Hand-raising** — transferred to a prepared **brooder**: a small enclosed heated unit lined with soft clean cloth, held at the target temperature.

## Chick rearing — parent-raised

- **Minimal human intervention.** The parents provide food, brood the chicks to keep them warm, and maintain the nest. The falconer observes via camera and intervenes only for illness or visible failure-to-thrive.
- **Advantages.** Chicks develop **normal species-recognition**, do not imprint on humans, and become psychologically normal falcons suitable for training, flying, and working. This is the method that produces working falconry birds.
- **Limitations.** Parent failures — insufficient feeding, abandonment, infanticide — can cost the whole clutch. Rare in experienced bonded pairs, occasional in first-season or stressed pairs. A breeder watching via camera can often intervene (remove the clutch for artificial incubation or pulled-chick hand-raising) if parent failure is detected early.
- **Ringing and microchipping.** Performed inside the nest when chicks are the right size — typically **day 10–14 for rings** and **day 21+ for microchips**. Done quickly, calmly, with minimal chamber disturbance. The breeder lifts the chicks out, works fast, and returns them to the parents.
- **Parent diet during rearing.** The tiercel and female need **ample, high-quality, well-hydrated food** throughout the chick-rearing window. Provisioning a rearing pair is roughly double the normal adult ration, and food hygiene is critical — parents feeding chicks contaminated food is a leading cause of chick loss.

## Chick rearing — hand-raised (creche or individual)

**Used for**: future imprint tiercels for AI programmes; birds destined for specialist training methods that work better from hand-raised baselines; rescue or fostered clutches where parents have failed; eggs from AI where no parents are available.

Hand-raising is straightforward in principle and unforgiving of error in practice. **Hand-reared chicks grow more slowly than parent-reared chicks** and take longer to reach each developmental milestone — an honest expectation to set.

### Feeding schedule by age

From the Peregrine Fund *Falcon Propagation* manual, adapted for the three species covered here:

- **Days 1–7 — newly hatched, eyes closed, sparse down.** Feeds **every 1–2 hours during daylight hours**. Small pieces of fresh lean meat (day-old chick breast, plucked and finely chopped — **no bone, no skin, no organ yet**). **6–10 % of body weight per feed.** Meat warmed to body temperature. Never feed cold, never feed overheated.
- **Days 7–14 — eyes open, pin feathers emerging.** Feeds **every 3–4 hours**. Larger pieces. Begin introducing **small bone fragments and a little skin** to build the natural cast.
- **Days 14–21 — feathers emerging, more alert, mobile within the brooder.** Feeds **every 4–6 hours**. Whole small day-old chicks roughly halved; the chick begins holding food and tearing.
- **Days 21–35 — fully feathered, approaching fledging, active.** **3–4 feeds per day**, transitioning toward self-feeding. Whole small prey items offered — the chick learns to deal with bone, feather, and skin.
- **Post-fledge (day 35 onwards).** **2 feeds per day**, whole prey, and weight management begins — a bird that will be flown or trained needs its working weight calibrated from this point forwards.

### Key notes that sit alongside the schedule

- **Food temperature.** Warmed to roughly body temperature (37–40 °C). Never cold — cold food slows gut motility and can cause crop stasis. Never hot — burns the crop.
- **Food quality.** Same standards as adult nutrition (see **Nutrition**). Captive-bred day-old chicks from a hygienic source are the backbone; freshly killed, hygienically handled, not previously frozen-and-thawed multiple times.
- **Feeding technique.** Offer small pieces on forceps or clean fingers, held in front of the chick's beak. The chick gapes and takes the food. **Never force-feed a chick on its back** — food goes down the windpipe and causes **aspiration pneumonia**, which is usually fatal.
- **Weight tracking.** Daily weights on a small digital scale, recorded. The Peregrine Fund manual publishes expected growth curves per species. Sudden weight stalls or losses indicate a problem — usually feeding technique, food quality, or incipient illness.
- **Brooder temperature.** Start at **35–37 °C** for the first week, reducing by roughly **1 °C per week** until the chick is at room temperature by around week four. Too warm and the chick pants and dehydrates; too cool and it cannot digest.
- **Brooder hygiene.** Clean cloth liner changed **daily at minimum**, twice daily in the first week. Damp or soiled bedding is the leading environmental cause of **aspergillosis in chicks** — and aspergillosis in a two-week-old chick is almost always fatal.
- **Common chick problems.** **Aspiration pneumonia** from feeding too fast or on the back. **Crop impaction** from overfeeding or feeding before the last meal has cleared the crop. **Aspergillosis** from damp or contaminated brooder conditions. **Failure to thrive** — usually a food-quality or technique issue; a vet should see any chick that stalls on weight for more than 24 hours.

## Fledging and first flights

- **When.** **Fledging** — the first real flight — happens around **35–45 days** depending on species. Gyrfalcons fledge slightly later than sakers and peregrines, reflecting their larger body and cooler-climate origin. Parent-raised chicks fledge on their own schedule from the nest; hand-raised chicks are moved to a flight chamber.
- **Flight chamber.** A larger enclosed space — bigger than a breeding chamber, designed for the fledgling to build wing strength with safe walls to collide with rather than being lost on a first wild flight. Typical flight chambers are several metres long with padded walls and simple perching.
- **Transition.** For parent-raised chicks, fledging happens naturally from the nest ledge into the chamber interior. For hand-raised chicks, the falconer moves them from the brooder to the flight chamber as they begin attempting longer flights within the brooder.
- **Independence.** By **60–90 days** most chicks are self-feeding, flying confidently, and ready to begin the early phases of training (see **Training**) or — for chicks destined for sale — transfer to their new keeper.

## Hybrid breeding — specifics

Hybrid falcons — deliberate crosses between species — are the unique Gulf specialty, and they have distinct considerations.

- **AI is effectively mandatory.** Cross-species pairs rarely bond; natural-pair hybrid production does happen but is uncommon and not a method anyone plans around. The standard approach is an **imprint tiercel** of one species and a **chamber female** of the other.
- **Imprint tiercel + chamber female** is the canonical hybrid method. The tiercel is imprinted and trained to the hat; the female is housed in a standard chamber and inseminated with AI during her receptive window.
- **Direction of cross matters.** A **gyr tiercel × saker female** is biologically different from a **saker tiercel × gyr female**. Mitochondrial DNA follows the female line; size asymmetry varies (female sakers are smaller than female gyrs); rearing dynamics differ. Most Gulf gyr × saker production uses the **gyr-tiercel / saker-female** direction.
- **Common Gulf crosses.**
  - **Gyr × Saker** — the flagship. Typically gyr tiercel with saker female. Combines gyrfalcon size and power with saker heat tolerance and temperament.
  - **Gyr × Peregrine** — rarer, more technically difficult, harder to produce consistently. Dramatic aerial birds when successful.
  - **Saker × Peregrine** — intermediate, less commonly produced than either of the above.
- **Fertility and viability.** Hybrids are **viable and often fertile in the first generation (F1)**. Later generations and backcrosses have **more variable fertility** — an F1 × F1 pairing may produce fertile or infertile offspring unpredictably.
- **Documentation.** Hybrid provenance — exact parent species, lineage of each parent, date and method of the cross, and any previous offspring of the same cross — matters commercially (buyers pay premiums for documented lines) and legally (CITES paperwork for hybrid movement is distinct from single-species documentation).

For which hybrid is worth producing for what purpose, see **Breeds & Types**.

## Breeding-specific health issues

Cross-references to the **Health & Prevention**, **Supplements & Vitamins**, and **Nutrition** chapters. These conditions are specific to this phase of the year:

- **Egg-bound female.** Life-threatening. The female cannot pass a formed egg — visible as listlessness, straining on the ledge, tail pumping, and unwillingness to move. **Calcium gluconate injection** by an avian vet is the standard response (see **Supplements & Vitamins**). **Often fatal if the vet visit is delayed** — this is a same-day emergency.
- **Soft-shell egg.** The female passes an egg with an inadequate or missing shell, usually from **calcium or vitamin D deficiency** in the pre-lay diet. Dietary correction for the next clutch; see **Nutrition** and **Supplements & Vitamins**.
- **Cloacal prolapse.** The cloaca protrudes after straining during laying or heavy incubation. **Vet emergency** — prompt reduction and management are necessary to avoid long-term damage.
- **Crop impaction in chicks.** Overfeeding, feeding before the crop has cleared, or food that is too large or cold. The crop remains swollen past normal clearing time. **Requires vet attention** — do not try to massage a severely impacted crop at home.
- **Aspiration pneumonia in chicks.** Food or fluid enters the windpipe during feeding, usually because the chick was fed on its back or fed too fast. **Often fatal.** The only remedy is prevention — correct upright feeding posture, unhurried delivery, small pieces.
- **Aspergillosis in chicks.** *Aspergillus fumigatus* spores germinate in warm damp brooder or incubator conditions. A chick with aspergillosis typically shows respiratory distress and fails rapidly. **Prevention via strict brooder hygiene** is the only reliable approach; treatment of an infected chick is rarely successful. See **Health & Prevention**.
- **Parent abandonment or infanticide.** Rare in well-bonded experienced pairs; occasional in young or stressed pairs. Often triggered by **human disturbance** of the chamber. Signs: parents stop brooding, ignore chicks, or in extreme cases injure them. The clutch must be pulled for artificial incubation or hand-raising if abandonment is detected.

## Post-season management

The breeding year does not end with fledging. The post-season is when the female rebuilds.

- **Feather moult.** Breeding females lose body condition during laying and incubation — both through calcium loss and through the metabolic cost of sitting. The post-season moult is the recovery. See **Housing & Environment** for the moulting chamber (**المقيض**) and **Supplements & Vitamins** for moult-recovery compounds.
- **Weight rebound.** Careful rebuilding, not rushing back to training or flying weight. A female pushed back into work before she has recovered enters the next breeding season in poorer condition.
- **When to retire a pair.** Productivity typically declines after **10–12 years** of age, faster for birds with any clinical history. Some breeders retire pairs when clutch size drops persistently or fertility rates fall — at that point the pair is relocated to retirement housing, not culled.
- **Fallow year.** Some experienced operations **rest a pair every 2–3 seasons** — deliberately skipping a breeding year — to extend total productive lifespan. Not universally practised, but worth considering for prized pairs.

## Records — what to track for every breeding attempt

These records become the documentation for each chick's captive-bred certificate and follow the bird across its entire life.

- **Pair identity and parent lineage** — IDs, ages, species, lineage of each parent; photos if possible.
- **Dates** — first copulation or AI, first egg, each subsequent egg, first external pip, each hatch, ringing, microchipping, fledging.
- **Egg measurements** — weight and dimensions of each egg if possible (useful for incubation humidity calibration).
- **Incubator logs** — temperature, humidity, turning, candling notes, weight change per egg.
- **Chick weights** — daily from hatch through fledging, per chick.
- **Feeding log** — for hand-raised chicks, the schedule and any deviations.
- **Health log** — any observation, any medical intervention, any medication, with dates.
- **Outcome** — each chick's disposition (kept, sold, died, transferred), with the receiving owner's details for any chick leaving the operation.

A planned **logbook feature** in a future phase of this guide will let keepers record all of the above online. Until then, a dedicated notebook or simple spreadsheet is the standard.

## Common mistakes — why most amateur breeding attempts fail

A short reference list. Most first-time attempts fail because of two or three of these at once.

- **Inadequate facility** — too small, insufficient privacy, wrong climate control. A breeding chamber is not a retrofitted mews.
- **Wrong pair selection** — incompatible temperaments, adults too young or too old, poor condition at the start of the season.
- **Incubator humidity or temperature drift** — the single most common technical failure. Narrow safe ranges; drift kills embryos silently.
- **Over-eager hand-feeding** — the cause of most chick losses. Aspiration pneumonia from feeding on the back; crop impaction from overfeeding; scalding from too-hot food.
- **Insufficient brooder hygiene** — damp or soiled bedding produces aspergillosis in chicks, which is effectively untreatable.
- **Missing legal paperwork** — offspring cannot legally change hands without CITES, captive-bred certificate, microchip, and a valid veterinary certificate. A clutch produced without the paperwork ready is a legally stranded clutch.
- **Under-estimating the imprint-tiercel investment** — 2+ years of intensive hand-rearing before the bird is breeding-age, and the bird is useless for anything else.
- **Rushing into breeding before mastering husbandry of adult falcons** — a keeper who cannot reliably maintain a flying saker at working weight is not ready to manage an incubator, a laying female, and a brooder simultaneously.

### Saker — breeding notes

The saker is **the most forgiving species to breed in Gulf conditions** — the species Gulf commercial operations often specialise in, because the saker thrives in captivity and pair-bonds readily.

**How the saker breeds:**
- **Natural pair bonding works well.** A bonded saker pair is the easiest of the three species to set up for natural-pair production. AI is optional rather than mandatory.
- **Clutch size.** 3–5 eggs is typical, with a documented range of 2–6 — the largest clutches of the three species on average.
- **Incubation.** **30–32 days** — the shortest incubation of the three.
- **Temperament during breeding.** Sakers tolerate the chamber environment well, do not stress easily from normal ambient disturbance, and recover quickly from the breeding season into moult.
- **Gulf commercial focus.** A very large share of serious Gulf production is saker-focused — single-species sakers for working falconry, and saker females as the dam side of gyr × saker hybrids.

**Saker-specific cautions:** calcium supplementation during pre-lay is important for laying-female sakers producing 4–5 egg clutches; the demand adds up. Breeding-chamber climate still matters but with a wider comfort band than gyrfalcons.

<SpeciesOnly species="peregrine">
### Peregrine — breeding notes

The peregrine is **moderately difficult** to breed. Natural pairs are possible and rewarding; AI is increasingly common because peregrine imprint tiercels are the foundation of the worldwide captive-breeding industry that emerged from the DDT conservation effort of the 1970s.

**How the peregrine breeds:**
- **Natural pair breeding works** for experienced, well-housed pairs — but peregrines are more stress-sensitive than sakers and facility quality matters more.
- **AI is the dominant commercial method** for peregrines worldwide, precisely because the technique originated with peregrine recovery and the species has been bred via imprint tiercels for fifty years.
- **Clutch size.** Around 4 eggs is typical, with a documented range of 3–5.
- **Incubation.** **33–35 days**, with a reported literature range of 30–36 days.
- **Fledging.** Around 38–45 days.
- **Temperament during breeding.** Peregrines settle into courtship more visibly than sakers — the bowing displays and food-passing are dramatic. But they also abandon or fail more readily than sakers under poor facility conditions or disturbance.

**Peregrine-specific cautions:** stress management during the breeding window is the single biggest variable. Camera monitoring rather than in-person visits, quiet nearby environment, no renovation work or other birds' noise near the chamber.
</SpeciesOnly>

<SpeciesOnly species="gyrfalcon">
### Gyrfalcon — breeding notes

The gyrfalcon is **the hardest species to breed successfully in Gulf conditions**. Climate, aspergillosis risk, and the longest incubation of the three species all combine to make this the breeding programme that demands the most infrastructure and the most experienced hands.

**How the gyrfalcon breeds:**
- **Climate control is not optional.** The chamber must hold cooler temperatures than a saker or peregrine chamber across the entire breeding window. The Gulf heat that a saker shrugs off will suppress a gyrfalcon pair's courtship entirely.
- **Aspergillosis risk dominates every decision.** Damp chambers, poor food hygiene, and unclean brooders produce fatal aspergillosis in gyrfalcon chicks more readily than in any other species. Prevention is the whole game.
- **Clutch size.** 2–4 eggs is typical, with a range of 1–5. The smallest clutches of the three species on average.
- **Incubation.** **About 35 days** — the longest of the three species and therefore the longest window during which an incubator failure can ruin a clutch.
- **Fledging.** Slightly later than sakers and peregrines, around 45–50 days.
- **White-morph breeding is especially delicate.** White-morph gyrfalcons are the highest-value birds in the Gulf market; their breeding programmes require the most careful pair selection, facility, and documentation. A single failed white-morph clutch is a significant commercial loss.
- **Hybrid tiercels.** Much of the gyrfalcon's role in Gulf breeding is as the **sire of hybrids** — the tiercel side of gyr × saker production. Imprint gyr tiercels are the centrepiece of that segment of the industry.

**Gyrfalcon-specific cautions:** weathering a breeding pair in summer is not advisable; the birds remain indoors under climate control. All the dry-air, clean-food, low-disturbance principles from **Housing & Environment** and **Health & Prevention** apply doubly during breeding and chick-rearing.
</SpeciesOnly>
