A fit working falcon fed a varied whole-prey diet needs **very little supplementation**. Supplementation is a **corrective tool for specific situations**, not a daily insurance policy. Over-supplementation is a real and well-documented cause of harm: fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) accumulate in the liver, calcium and phosphorus throw each other out of balance, and stacking products with overlapping ingredients silently triples a dose.

This chapter is a general reference. Switch the species tab for per-falcon notes, and switch the **mode** tab between organic feed-additive products and pharmaceutical (vet-prescribed) substances. Both are presented; only one set is appropriate for any given situation.

## When supplementation actually helps

There are seven situations in which most falconers and falcon hospitals agree some form of supplementation is warranted:

1. **Moult** (3–6 weeks of high feather and protein turnover) — multivitamin/mineral powder, amino-acid blend, vitamin E
2. **Heavy training or hunting season** — vitamin E + selenium for muscle support; B-complex
3. **Breeding females** — calcium (egg laying is a calcium drain)
4. **Hand-raised chicks** — daily calcium powder and a balanced vitamin/mineral additive while the skeleton ossifies
5. **Post-illness recovery** — probiotics after antibiotics, B-complex for appetite and nervous system
6. **Long-distance transport, quarantine, or new-bird stress** — probiotics, electrolyte support
7. **Specific deficiencies diagnosed by a vet** — targeted, time-limited

Outside these situations, a healthy adult falcon on a clean varied whole-prey diet generally needs **nothing**. Adding more is not a courtesy — it is a risk.

> This content is for informational purposes only. The substances and dosages discussed below are guideline references from published sources. The right product, dose, and duration for your specific bird must come from a licensed veterinarian.

## The supplement catalog

<ModeOnly mode="organic">

### Organic — feed additives, oral supplements, natural products

![A small wooden scoop sprinkling supplement powder onto fresh meat](/images/supplements/dosing.png)

These are the products you can use at home, sprinkled on food or added to water, without a prescription. Even so, none of them are "give freely". Each one is a tool for a specific job.

#### 1. Whole prey (the foundation)

The single best supplement is a **clean, varied whole-prey diet**: quail as the staple, day-old chicks in rotation, occasional rat or rabbit, the occasional feather or fur for casting. If this baseline is right, most other supplements become unnecessary.

#### 2. Calcium powder

**What it does.** Provides the calcium that growing bones, laying eggs, and stressed-skeleton birds need beyond what whole prey alone gives. Often combined with vitamin D3 to enable absorption.

**When to give.** Daily for hand-raised chicks. Daily during the lead-up to and through egg laying for breeding females. As a short-term boost (1–2 weeks) after fractures or skeletal stress, on vet advice.

**Common products.** Repashy Calcium Plus, Nekton MSA, Zolcal-D (liquid). Choose a raptor- or bird-formulated product, not a human or dog one — the calcium-to-phosphorus ratio is different.

**How.** Sprinkle a small pinch (vet-specified for the bird's weight) onto slightly moist food so it sticks. Dry meat sheds the powder.

**Caution.** Stop when the situation passes. Long-term over-calciation in adult birds creates its own kidney problems.

#### 3. Raptor multivitamin/mineral powder

**What it does.** A balanced general-purpose vitamin (A, D3, E, K, B-complex) and trace-mineral top-up formulated for birds of prey.

**When to give.** During moult; during recovery; during sustained heavy hunting/training. Typically a few times a week, not daily.

**Common products.** Vitahawk (Northwoods Falconry); Bird Care Co Raptor Essentials. Both are widely used in working falconry and explicitly raptor-formulated.

**How.** Sprinkled on food per the manufacturer's stated frequency.

**Caution.** Do not stack with a separate moulting blend — they overlap heavily and double the fat-soluble vitamins.

#### 4. Moulting blend

**What it does.** Concentrated mix targeted at feather growth — extra amino acids (lysine, methionine, cysteine) plus vitamins for the higher protein turnover of the moult.

**When to give.** During the moult only — typically 3–6 weeks per main feather group. Stop afterwards.

**Common products.** Vetafarm Falcon Moulting Aid is the explicitly falcon-labelled option; some falconers use a general raptor multivitamin during moult instead.

**How.** Per manufacturer dose, on food.

**Caution.** As above — don't run it alongside a general raptor multivitamin during the same period.

#### 5. Probiotics

**What it does.** Restores and maintains the gut bacterial population. Particularly important after a course of antibiotics (which wipe out beneficial bacteria along with the targets), after stress, and in young birds whose gut flora is still establishing.

**When to give.** For 1–2 weeks after any antibiotic course. During and after transport, quarantine, or any sustained stress event. Optionally as a low-level daily preventive in young birds.

**Common products.** Avipro Plus is the most commonly used falconry-suitable formulation. Generic *Lactobacillus*/*Bifidobacterium* avian blends are acceptable.

**How.** Mixed into drinking water per product instructions, or sprinkled on slightly moist food.

**Caution.** Refrigerate after opening — live bacteria die at room temperature. Check the expiry date.

#### 6. Vitamin E + selenium

**What it does.** Supports muscle function and recovery in heavy work. Selenium works with vitamin E as an antioxidant; the two are usually given together.

**When to give.** During heavy training/hunting season and during breeding. Typically 2–3 times per week, not daily.

**Common products.** Many raptor multivitamins already include both — check before stacking. Standalone vitamin E + selenium is available as veterinary product.

**Caution.** Selenium has a narrow safe range. Do not exceed manufacturer dose. If your raptor multivitamin already contains it, do not add a separate dose.

#### 7. B-complex (oral)

**What it does.** Supports appetite, nervous system function, and recovery. The B vitamins are water-soluble (excess is excreted), so safer than fat-soluble vitamins, but still no need to give daily without reason.

**When to give.** During recovery from illness, after anorexia, in birds returning to weight after a setback.

**Common products.** Found in nearly every raptor multivitamin. Standalone B-complex liquid is available.

#### 8. Bee pollen

**What it does.** Traditional moult-support additive — protein, amino acids, trace minerals.

**When to give.** Optional addition during moult.

**Caution.** Evidence is largely anecdotal in falconry. Useful as a small supplement, not a substitute for a proper moulting blend or multivitamin. Source from a clean apiary.

#### 9. Apple cider vinegar (in water)

**What it does.** Traditional gut tonic; mild antimicrobial. Acidifies water slightly which discourages bacterial growth.

**When to give.** Optionally as a low-level water additive (a few drops per litre) — most useful in summer when standing water spoils faster.

**Caution.** Evidence level is low; this is folk practice with some plausibility, not a pharmacological treatment. Use raw unfiltered ACV. Don't use as a substitute for clean water changes.

#### 10. Glucose / honey

**What it does.** Rapid energy source for an exhausted, hypoglycaemic, or recovering bird.

**When to give.** Vet-controlled emergency use. A weak, cold, just-recovered bird may benefit from a small amount of glucose solution; honey is the home-substitute when nothing else is available.

**Caution.** Not a routine supplement. Excess sugar in a healthy bird is harmful.

### Pharmaceutical — vet-prescribed substances

<PharmaceuticalWarning />

![Three glass injection vials and a syringe on a cream cloth](/images/supplements/pharmaceutical.png)

The substances below are **prescription items**. They are documented here so that when your vet uses them you understand what is being given and why — not so you administer them yourself. Doses are intentionally not given as numbers; doses are species-, weight-, and condition-specific.

#### 1. Duphalyte multivitamin injection

**What it is.** A combined fluid, electrolyte, B-vitamin and amino-acid injectable solution used as supportive care for debilitated, dehydrated, or recovering birds.

**When a vet uses it.** Anorexia, dehydration, post-surgery, severe illness recovery. Often given alongside fluid therapy.

**How given.** Subcutaneous or intravenous, by a vet.

#### 2. Vitamin AD3E injectable

**What it is.** A high-concentration injectable form of fat-soluble vitamins A, D3, and E.

**When a vet uses it.** Diagnosed deficiency, recovery from severe illness, certain cases of poor feather quality unresponsive to oral supplementation.

**Caution.** Fat-soluble vitamin overdose by injection is harder to reverse than oral. Used sparingly.

#### 3. B-complex injectable

**What it is.** Concentrated water-soluble B vitamins for rapid effect.

**When a vet uses it.** Severe anorexia, neurological signs, sudden weight loss, post-illness when oral intake is unreliable.

#### 4. Calcium gluconate injection

**What it is.** Injectable calcium for emergency hypocalcaemia — most often a laying female that crashes from acute calcium drain (egg-binding seizures).

**When a vet uses it.** **Emergency only.** Seizures, tremor, or weakness in a laying or post-laying female; suspected hypocalcaemia.

#### 5. Iron + B12 injectable

**What it is.** Combined treatment for anaemia.

**When a vet uses it.** Documented anaemia (heavy parasite burden, blood loss, chronic disease).

#### 6. Antifungals — itraconazole, voriconazole

**What they are.** Prescription antifungal drugs used for **aspergillosis** treatment and (in high-risk situations) prophylaxis.

**When a vet uses them.** Confirmed aspergillosis, or as prophylaxis in a high-risk bird (gyrfalcon entering Gulf summer with environmental exposure history). Long courses, regular blood monitoring.

**Caution.** Liver-toxic at high doses. Vet supervision is non-negotiable.

#### 7. Antiparasitics

**What they are.** Different drugs for different parasites:

- **Toltrazuril** — for coccidia (intestinal protozoa)
- **Fenbendazole, praziquantel** — for nematode and tapeworm worms
- **Permethrin, fipronil** — for external parasites (lice, mites, hippoboscid flies); raptor-appropriate dose only
- **Ivermectin** — broader-spectrum, vet-controlled

**When a vet uses them.** After faecal screening or visible parasite identification.

**Caution.** Wrong dose is toxic. Permethrin in particular has narrow safety in birds; never use cat or dog products.

#### 8. Electrolyte / fluid therapy

**What it is.** Lactated Ringer's solution and similar balanced electrolyte fluids.

**When a vet uses it.** Dehydration, shock, surgery support, heat stroke.

**How.** Subcutaneous or intravenous administration.

#### 9. Antibiotics — a note

Antibiotics are not supplements and are not catalogued here. They are diagnosis-driven treatments prescribed for specific identified infections. The relevant point for a supplement chapter is that **after any antibiotic course, organic probiotics should be given for 1–2 weeks** to restore gut flora.

## Storage and sourcing

- **Buy raptor-specific products.** Human and dog supplements have wrong concentrations and often toxic ratios for falcons. The price difference is small; the safety difference is large.
- **Refrigerate where the product specifies.** Live probiotics, opened liquid vitamins, and some moulting blends die or oxidise at room temperature. Check the label.
- **Watch expiration dates.** Vitamins lose potency; fats go rancid; live cultures die.
- **Don't decant into unlabeled containers.** Two months later you won't remember which one was which.
- **Source from established avian-veterinary suppliers.** Cheap unbranded multivitamins of unknown manufacture are not worth the risk.

## Common mistakes to avoid

1. **Stacking multivitamins.** Two products with the same ingredient = double the dose. The most common silent overdose, especially with vitamin A (which is also high in mice).
2. **Mouse-heavy diet + vitamin A supplement.** Mice are already very high in vitamin A. Adding a multivitamin with A on top compounds the toxicity.
3. **Continuing supplements indefinitely.** A moulting blend after the moult is over is just expensive vitamin overdose. Stop when the situation passes.
4. **Sprinkling powder on dry meat.** It falls off uneaten. Lightly moisten the food first so the powder adheres.
5. **Mixing organic and pharmaceutical without a vet.** Some combinations interfere with each other (antifungals + certain vitamins; antibiotics + probiotics if given the same hour, etc.).
6. **Treating supplementation as a substitute for diagnosis.** A bird that's "off" doesn't need a multivitamin — it needs a vet examination. Supplements treat known situations, not unknown problems.

## Tendencies by species

The supplements above apply to all three falcons. What differs is **how much they tolerate, how reactive they are, and which extra needs come up**. Switch the species tabs at the top to see the specific notes.

<SpeciesOnly species="saker">
### Saker — supplement notes

The saker is the **most resilient** of the three on the supplement front. A clean varied diet covers most of its needs across the season.

**Where supplementation typically matters most:**
- **Breeding females** — calcium during egg-laying, as for any falcon, but the larger-bodied saker female needs proportionally more
- **Moult** — standard multivitamin or moulting blend
- **Otherwise minimal** — the saker's broader diet tolerance means it's the least likely of the three to develop a deficiency in normal husbandry

**What to watch:** because sakers tolerate occasional rodents, the vitamin A concern (mice are high in A) is most relevant here. Avoid stacking vitamin A supplements on a rodent-rotation diet.
</SpeciesOnly>

<SpeciesOnly species="peregrine">
### Peregrine — supplement notes

The peregrine is **smaller, more reactive, and less forgiving of dosing errors**. Precision matters more here than in either of the other two.

**Where supplementation typically matters most:**
- **Heavy training season** — vitamin E + selenium for muscle support; smaller absolute doses than the larger species
- **Recovery from any setback** — probiotics and B-complex; the peregrine's smaller margin means recovery support pays off quickly
- **Stress events** — peregrines crash from over-handling alone; probiotics and electrolyte support during stress are worth more in this species than in the saker

**What to watch:** dose by **bird weight, not "one product, one dose"**. The peregrine female at 900 g and the saker female at 1200 g should not get the same scoop of powder. Read the manufacturer's per-kg guidance, not the per-bird guidance.
</SpeciesOnly>

<SpeciesOnly species="gyrfalcon">
### Gyrfalcon — supplement notes

The gyrfalcon's supplement profile is **driven by aspergillosis risk and heat stress** in Gulf conditions. The supplement strategy supports these specific vulnerabilities.

**Where supplementation typically matters most:**
- **Aspergillosis prevention** — supplements alone do **not** prevent aspergillosis (clean dry mews and food hygiene do). But during high-risk windows (entering Gulf summer with prior exposure), vet-supervised antifungal prophylaxis on the pharmaceutical tab may be considered. This is a vet decision, not a home one.
- **Heat stress support** — electrolyte support during summer training; vitamin E for muscle stress in heat; cooler housing and bathing matter more than any supplement
- **Vitamin E** — particularly relevant for the gyr's larger muscle mass under stress

**What to watch:** the pharmaceutical tab is more relevant for gyrfalcons than for the other two species, *only* under vet direction. Self-medicating a gyrfalcon with antifungals because of aspergillosis worry is a recipe for liver damage on top of every other risk.
</SpeciesOnly>
