![Interior of a modern falcon moulting chamber with a skylight, shelf perch, bath bowl, and a single leather hood on a side ledge](/images/housing/moulting-chamber.png)

Housing is the silent partner in falconry. A bird that is well-fed, well-handled, and well-trained but poorly housed will still decline. Nothing in the nutrition, health, or supplements chapters compensates for the wrong environment — damp air gives aspergillosis, the wrong perch gives bumblefoot, a hot midday block burns feet.

Two distinct physical spaces carry the year, plus a third that bridges them:

- **The daily room (غرفة الطيور)** — where the bird lives most of the year
- **The moulting chamber (المقيض)** — a separate, cooler, quieter space for summer and the moult
- **The weathering yard** — the outdoor perching area that links them both to sun and fresh air

The Gulf climate is the defining constraint for every decision in this chapter. Everything below is oriented to that reality.

## The daily room (غرفة الطيور)

![A clean, well-ventilated traditional daily room with a padded perch](/images/health/clean-mews.png)

The daily room is the year-round living space — where the bird is weighed, fed, observed, and rested outside of training and weathering time. It is not a cage. It is a room, built and maintained to a falcon's body rather than a human's.

### Dimensions

A working minimum for a single falcon is roughly **a room large enough for the bird to extend its wings in every direction without touching a wall, perch, or fixture** — for a saker or gyrfalcon that typically means at least 2 × 2 metres of floor, 2.5 metres of ceiling height. Bigger is better if the climate control keeps up. Crowded rooms concentrate droppings, humidity, and stress.

### Walls and floor

- **Smooth, easily cleaned surfaces.** Painted plaster or smooth concrete. Avoid porous block, rough stone, or unfinished wood — parasites, mould, and bacterial biofilms live in crevices.
- **Pale neutral colours** — makes droppings visible so they get cleaned, and keeps the room calm.
- **Floor of fine sand or smooth concrete**, cleanable daily. Sand is forgiving underfoot; concrete drains cleanest.
- **No sharp corners at bird height** — a stressed bird will fly into them.

### Perches

The single most important piece of fixed equipment in the room.

- **Block perch** — the classic: a padded top (Astroturf or wrapped fabric) on a low stable base. Used for most of the day.
- **Shelf perch** — a padded shelf mounted on the wall at bird height, giving variety of position.
- **Bow perch** — less common for falcons (more typical for hawks), but usable.
- **Padding** — Astroturf is the standard for a reason: soft enough for daily standing, textured enough to discourage slipping, cleanable.
- **Diameter matters.** A perch too thin forces the foot to grip too tightly; too wide and the foot never fully closes. Match it to the species.
- **Spacing** — any second perch in the same room should be far enough that the bird cannot reach the other when tethered.

### Lighting

- **Natural daylight is best**, filtered through small high windows rather than direct sun.
- **No direct noon sun on the bird's perch** — heat stress and feather bleaching.
- **A dawn-to-dusk cycle** supports normal behaviour; avoid bright artificial light at night.

### Ventilation

This is where aspergillosis is prevented or not prevented.

- **Constant gentle airflow, no drafts.** Air moves through the room; the bird does not sit in a breeze.
- **Dry air.** Humidity is the *Aspergillus* spore's best friend — see the Climate section and the Health chapter.
- **Exchange old air for new every day** — an open grille or vent.
- **No stagnant corners** where mouldy food or droppings can sit unseen.

### Water

![A shallow stone bath bowl of clean water on warm sand, a single feather floating](/images/bath-bowl.png)

- **Shallow bath bowl** — wide, low-sided, easy for the bird to step in and out of. Offered two to three times a week, more often in summer.
- **Fresh drinking water** always available in a separate smaller bowl. Change it daily; in Gulf summer, twice a day.

### Temperature and humidity

In Gulf summer the daily room is air-conditioned or cooled by evaporation. A rough working target is **22–28 °C at bird height** with humidity held below 60 %. The Climate section below covers the tools for this.

### Daily cleaning

Five minutes every morning. The full checklist is in the Daily Care chapter — droppings cleared, perches wiped, water refreshed, ventilation checked, floor swept.

## The moulting chamber (المقيض)

For about three months each summer — when Gulf heat is at its worst and the bird is shedding and regrowing feathers — the working falcon transitions into a dedicated space: **the المقيض**, the moulting chamber. This is not the daily room with a label change. It is a different kind of space for a different kind of life.

### Why a separate space exists

The moult is a high-energy, high-protein process that takes the bird out of working condition for weeks. During it the bird needs:

- **Minimal handling** — every interruption extends the moult
- **Full unrestricted rations** — no weight pressure for hunting
- **A calm, cool, dim environment** that supports the body's effort
- **Zero stress** — stress hormones suppress feather growth

Putting a moulting bird in its daily room works against every one of these. The المقيض is purpose-built to provide them.

### Design differences from the daily room

- **Dimmer** — soft diffused light through a single skylight or small high window. The bird should be able to see clearly but not feel on display. Think "quiet reading room", not "cave".
- **Cooler** — actively climate-controlled. The target is lower than the daily room, typically **20–24 °C** in Gulf summer. This is the defining reason the chamber exists at all.
- **More enclosed** — fewer sight lines out, fewer visual stimulations. The bird turns inward for the moult.
- **Single perch** — one well-designed perch, centered. No choice architecture; the bird rests and eats there.
- **Shallow bath bowl** — essential. Bathing supports moult feather condition. Offered 2–3 times a week.
- **Minimal furniture, no decoration** — what you don't put in the room is as important as what you do.
- **Single leather hood on a side ledge** — not for use, but as a psychological marker: the bird's equipment is there, the bird's partnership with the falconer is intact, this is a dignified rest, not exile.

### When the bird transitions in

When the first summer heat hits and the first old feathers begin to drop — typically **late spring through early summer**, varying by species and individual. Don't force the transition before the bird is ready; an early move to the chamber without the moult starting just wastes space and bores the bird.

### Life inside

- **Daily observation only** — look from the doorway, check food and water and droppings, leave.
- **Food delivered without ceremony** — on the shelf or in a feeding tray, full rations.
- **Weighing relaxed** — weekly rather than daily, and only enough to confirm the bird is eating well.
- **Bathing 2–3 times a week** — essential for feather quality.
- **No training, no handling** beyond what's strictly necessary.

Multiply this by twelve weeks and the bird emerges with fresh plumage, full condition, and ready for the training run-up into hunting season.

### When the bird transitions out

When the moult is visibly complete — all new feathers fully grown in, no pins remaining, plumage sleek. Re-manning and reconditioning happen back in the daily room and the weathering yard, not in the chamber.

### Common mistakes

- **Too dark.** "Dim" is not "pitch black". Birds in near-darkness become disoriented and develop behavioural issues.
- **Too warm.** If the chamber isn't noticeably cooler than the daily room, it's defeating its own purpose.
- **Too much disturbance.** Well-meaning daily visits to "check on the bird" reset the calm the chamber is meant to provide.
- **Inadequate food hygiene.** The bird's immune system is already under moult load; poor food hygiene during this window is the most common aspergillosis trigger in captive falcons. Every food-prep rule from the Nutrition chapter applies doubly here.
- **Skipping the chamber entirely.** Running a bird through the moult in the daily room is possible but usually produces slower, more stressful, and lower-quality moults.

## The weathering yard

![A weathering yard with multiple wooden perches under canvas shade panels](/images/housing/weathering-yard.png)

The weathering yard is the outdoor perching area — the third space that bridges the daily room and the training field. A bird that spends all its time indoors loses condition; a bird that weathers daily stays sleek, alert, and behaviourally stable.

### Purpose

- **Sun on the plumage** — supports natural oil production and feather condition
- **Fresh airflow** — cannot be faked indoors
- **Mental stimulation** — visual variety, weather, distant sounds
- **Natural temperature regulation** — the bird self-adjusts rather than being held at a single AC setpoint

See the Daily Care chapter for weathering *routine* (how long, how often). This section is about the *setup*.

### Layout

- **Perch spacing.** Any two perches must be far enough apart that a tethered bird on one cannot reach the other. Falcons threatened by a neighbour will not relax.
- **Sight-line management.** Birds should be able to see out across the yard but not see humans or dogs moving behind them.
- **Clear space around each perch** — no walls, fences, or obstructions within strike range of an upset bird.

### Shade structures

In Gulf climate, shade is not an amenity — it is structural. Canvas panels on timber frames, as shown in the photograph, are the Gulf standard:

- **Filters harsh sun** without creating a sealed hot box
- **Allows airflow** underneath
- **Easy to raise, lower, or relocate** seasonally

Without shade, midday weathering is impossible for half the year.

### Ground material

- **Raked sand** — traditional, soft, absorbs droppings, easy to rake and refresh
- **Pea gravel** — alternative with better drainage
- **Not grass** — holds moisture, harbours parasites, doesn't survive tethered weight

### Security

- **Tethered with a rated leash and a working swivel** — see Daily Care for equipment detail
- **Barriers against dogs, children, other predators** — a wall or fence around the yard itself
- **Always within supervision** — a tethered bird left unsupervised can catch itself on the leash or be attacked. No exceptions.

### Gulf-summer adjustments

As with Daily Care's rule: **weathering hours shift to early morning and late afternoon**. Midday weathering in June–August is not "weather carefully", it is "do not weather". The Climate section below covers cooling techniques for the yard if morning/evening windows are insufficient.

## Climate management — the Gulf overlay

Every housing decision above is conditioned by the Gulf climate. For half the year, the challenge is heat and dust; for the other half, relative winter cool. Humidity is year-round, and it is the silent killer.

### Summer heat

- **Air conditioning** in the daily room and المقيض — the practical baseline for serious falcon keeping in the Gulf
- **Evaporative cooling** (wet mats, fine mist, wetted floors) — supplement to AC, useful in the weathering yard
- **Shade** everywhere outdoors — canvas, timber, mesh
- **Airflow** — fans on low, moving air across the bird's height
- **Timing** — shift outdoor activity to dawn and dusk

### Humidity

- **Target under 60 %** in enclosed spaces, lower if possible
- **Dehumidifier** in the daily room and chamber if your climate regularly exceeds target
- **Dry bedding and perches always** — see the Health chapter on aspergillosis
- **Why it matters more than absolute cool** — *Aspergillus fumigatus* spores germinate in warm humid conditions, not in warm dry conditions. A slightly warmer but dry room is safer than a cooler humid one.

### Winter

- **Relatively easy** — most Gulf winter temperatures suit falcons well
- **Still needs airflow and dryness** — fog and damp winter nights are aspergillosis windows
- **No heating required** for healthy adult falcons unless temperatures drop unusually low

### Monitoring

- **Thermometer and humidity gauge** at bird height, not at ceiling height — the two readings differ meaningfully
- **Daily glance at both readings** — becomes a five-second habit
- **The "is it comfortable at bird height" test** — if you would find it stuffy sitting on the floor of the room, so would the bird

## Multi-bird housing

![A traditional folding wooden cadge with leather seat for carrying multiple falcons](/images/housing/cadge.png)

Keeping more than one falcon multiplies almost every housing decision above. Two birds in one daily room is not "twice the work"; it's the work, plus quarantine, plus cross-contamination risk, plus stress management.

### Separation is essential

- **Separate daily rooms** are strongly preferred. One room per bird is the working standard for serious falcon keeping.
- **If two birds must share**, physical separation (a partition, a wall, a distance greater than any possible reach) is mandatory. They should not be able to see each other at rest.
- **Separate bath bowls and water bowls** always — disease transmission route.

### The cadge

The cadge is a portable padded perching frame used to:
- **Transport multiple birds** over distance (vehicle, field)
- **Briefly hold several birds** in a staging area
- **Free up the falconer's hands** while handling another bird

It is **not a long-term housing solution** — birds on a cadge are on display to each other and to the environment, which is stressful over more than a few hours. Use it for transport and transition, not for daily life.

### Hygiene between birds

- **Wash hands between birds.** Every time.
- **Glove and equipment disinfection** if you're handling known-ill or newly-arrived birds.
- **Separate cleaning tools** for each room — one broom per daily room ideally.

See the Health chapter for the full cross-contamination protocol.

### Quarantine for new birds

**A new bird is quarantined for at least 30 days** before it enters the main housing or comes into contact with resident birds. During that 30 days:

- Separate air space (different building if possible)
- Faecal screen at start and end
- Full vet check before integration
- The falconer handles the quarantined bird **last** each day, not first

This is the single most important biosecurity practice in a multi-bird setup. It costs you a month; it saves you a flock.

### Noise and stress

Multiple birds hear each other, smell each other, and sense each other even through walls. The cumulative stress of a poorly-designed multi-bird setup is not always obvious in any one bird but shows up in collective flight performance, moult quality, and disease resistance. Fewer birds, better housed, is almost always better than more birds, crowded.

## Common housing mistakes

- **Damp bedding, poor ventilation.** The number-one cause of aspergillosis in captive Gulf falcons. See the Health chapter.
- **Wrong perch material or diameter.** Classic bumblefoot setup — rough surface, wrong size, daily standing. See the Health chapter.
- **Insufficient shade in the weathering yard.** Heat stress and feet burns from hot perch surfaces.
- **Too-small daily room.** Behavioural issues, stress, inability to stretch wings, plumage damage.
- **Moulting chamber too bright, too warm, or too disturbed.** Incomplete moults, broken feathers, recurring illness.
- **Mixing new birds with resident birds without quarantine.** Fast route to disease transmission across the whole collection.
- **Stacking perches close together.** Birds that can see or reach each other do not settle; chronic stress.
- **Using human or livestock disinfectants without checking raptor safety.** Many common disinfectants are toxic to birds at active concentrations.

## Tendencies by species

The housing above applies to all three falcons. What differs is **how tight the climate control has to be, how much stimulation the bird tolerates, and how expensive the system is to run well**. Switch the species tabs above to see your bird.

<SpeciesOnly species="saker">
### Saker — housing notes

The saker is the **most forgiving of housing variability** of the three. A standard Gulf falconry setup — well-ventilated daily room, summer chamber, shaded weathering yard — runs a saker comfortably across the year.

**Where the setup matters:**
- **Perch surface temperature in summer** — heavier body + hot perch = bumblefoot risk (see Health). Check perch surfaces before placing the bird.
- **Standard climate control is enough.** The saker tolerates the wider comfort band that kills gyrfalcons.
- **Cheapest of the three to house well.** A serious saker setup can be built and run with less specialised climate gear than a gyrfalcon setup demands.

This is the species for which the Gulf falconry tradition was, in many ways, designed.
</SpeciesOnly>

<SpeciesOnly species="peregrine">
### Peregrine — housing notes

The peregrine is the **most stress-sensitive** of the three. Housing decisions that a saker shrugs off — background noise, frequent visitors, close-quarters multi-bird — visibly affect a peregrine's condition and behaviour.

**Where the setup matters:**
- **Quieter environment preferred.** Lower-traffic location, fewer sight lines to household activity, less ambient noise.
- **Smaller daily room is acceptable**, but it must be meticulously clean and calm. A perfectly kept 2 × 2 m room suits a peregrine better than a distracted 4 × 4 m one.
- **Visual stimulation should be calmer** than for a saker. A peregrine sees movement and reacts; design accordingly.
- **Multi-bird setups need extra distance and partition** — a peregrine will not relax with another bird in visual range.

This is the species where the *quality* of the housing matters more than the size.
</SpeciesOnly>

<SpeciesOnly species="gyrfalcon">
### Gyrfalcon — housing notes

The gyrfalcon's housing requirements in the Gulf are the most demanding and most expensive to meet properly. Climate control is not optional and not negotiable; the gyrfalcon does not tolerate the wider comfort band that sakers and peregrines manage.

**Where the setup matters:**
- **Climate control is non-negotiable.** AC plus humidity control in the daily room, lower target temperatures in the chamber, more airflow everywhere. Running these systems across a Gulf summer is a significant operating cost.
- **The moulting chamber (المقيض) is the centrepiece of the year.** For a gyrfalcon keeper, the chamber's design and climate system are the biggest single housing investment. Get this wrong and you lose birds.
- **Aspergillosis prevention dominates every decision.** Dry air, clean food, clean surfaces, low disturbance. The whole housing system is oriented around keeping this disease out. See the Health chapter.
- **Weathering windows shrink in summer.** Early morning and late afternoon only; the rest of the day the bird must be inside under climate control.
- **Most expensive housing to run properly** — budget accordingly before acquiring the bird.

Housing a gyrfalcon well in the Gulf is serious work. Housing one badly costs the bird.
</SpeciesOnly>
