Falcons are obligate carnivores. Their bodies are built around digesting whole vertebrate prey — meat, organs, bone, and a small amount of feather or fur for roughage. Get the diet right and most other health questions take care of themselves. Get it wrong and even an otherwise well-housed bird will decline within weeks.

The whole-prey rule is non-negotiable: muscle meat alone, even high-quality, lacks the calcium, organ-derived vitamins, and roughage a falcon needs.

This chapter covers the three primary falcon species — **saker, peregrine, gyrfalcon**. Sections that differ by species are marked with the selector below.

## Best foods, ranked

The list below is roughly the order most working falconers and the major falcon hospitals reach for first.

### 1. Quail

![Coturnix quail](/images/nutrition/quail.png)

The closest thing to a "perfect" food for falcons. Whole, captive-bred coturnix quail provides a balanced ratio of protein, fat, calcium (from whole bone), and vitamins. The Abu Dhabi and Souq Waqif falcon hospitals both feed whole quail as the staple. Aim for healthy birds raised on clean feed — a quail is only as good as what it ate.

<SpeciesOnly species="peregrine">
**Peregrines** thrive on quail above all else — it most closely matches their natural bird-only diet. Use it as the daily staple.
</SpeciesOnly>

<SpeciesOnly species="gyrfalcon">
**Gyrfalcons** also do best on quail, but in Gulf summer prefer the leanest birds you can source — high fat reserves work against active cooling in heat.
</SpeciesOnly>

### 2. Day-old chicks

![Day-old chick](/images/nutrition/day-old-chick.png)

A practical, widely available staple, especially good for smaller falcons. They are slightly low in calcium relative to whole adult prey (the skeleton hasn't fully ossified) and slightly high in fat from the residual yolk sac, so they should not be the only food but are excellent in rotation. Remove the yolk sac before feeding if the bird is overweight or moulting heavily.

<SpeciesOnly species="gyrfalcon">
**Gyrfalcons** — the residual yolk sac fat is more of a concern. Routinely remove it; pair chicks with quail rather than feeding chick-only days.
</SpeciesOnly>

### 3. Rats

![Rat](/images/nutrition/rat.png)

Useful in rotation as a richer red-meat option. Use captive-bred frozen-thawed stock from a reputable supplier. **Mice are explicitly less suitable** as a regular food: very high vitamin A, high fat, and relatively low protein, so they should not be a primary food.

<SpeciesOnly species="peregrine">
**Peregrines** are bird specialists — use rats only occasionally, and never as the main food.
</SpeciesOnly>

<SpeciesOnly species="saker">
**Sakers** take rodents in the wild and tolerate them best. Comfortable as a regular rotation item.
</SpeciesOnly>

### 4. Rabbit

![Rabbit](/images/nutrition/rabbit.png)

Excellent lean red meat with good roughage from the fur. Use as variety, not as a daily staple — the calcium-to-phosphorus balance from rabbit alone is not ideal long-term.

## Foods to avoid

- **Pigeons and doves.** A hard rule, and the one most often broken. Pigeons can carry _Trichomonas gallinae_ (frounce/canker), falcon herpesvirus, and tuberculosis — diseases that can wipe out a working bird. If you must feed pigeon, use only birds you raised yourself in a closed loft, and freeze the meat at −20 °C for at least two weeks first.
- **Road-kill or wild-caught prey of unknown history.** Risk of pesticide residue, lead-shot fragments, and parasites.
- **Animals euthanised with barbiturates.** Pentobarbital persists in tissue and has caused fatal poisoning in falcons fed euthanised carcasses.
- **Spent racing pigeons** — same disease reasons as above.
- **Cooked meat, processed meat, dog or cat food.** Cooking destroys key nutrients, and the salt and preservatives in pet food are toxic at falcon body sizes.
- **Mice as the sole food source** (fine in rotation only, see above).

## Feeding frequency and ration

Adult working falcons are generally fed **once a day**. Young growing birds and birds in heavy moult need daily feeding; fit adult sport birds are sometimes given a smaller ration on training days and a fuller crop on rest days.

The right number is whatever maintains your bird's flying weight while keeping plumage, casting, and behaviour healthy. Below are starting references per species.

<SpeciesOnly species="saker">
**Saker** *(Falco cherrug, typical adult 730–1300 g)*

- Daily ration: **roughly 8–12% of body weight**
- Generalist diet — comfortable with quail + chicks + occasional rat
- Watch fat in summer: bumblefoot risk on hot perches is higher with overweight sakers
</SpeciesOnly>

<SpeciesOnly species="peregrine">
**Peregrine** *(Falco peregrinus, typical adult 600–1100 g)*

- Daily ration: **roughly 10–15% of body weight**
- Bird specialist — quail and day-old chick suit best; minimise rodent
- Smaller crop than saker or gyr — split larger rations across two smaller meals on heavy training days
</SpeciesOnly>

<SpeciesOnly species="gyrfalcon">
**Gyrfalcon** *(Falco rusticolus, typical adult 800–2100 g)*

- Daily ration: **roughly 6–10% of body weight**
- Cold-climate origin — least heat-tolerant of the three; ration must support active cooling
- Lower fat tolerance than saker; prefer leaner protein
- Highest aspergillosis risk of the three in Gulf conditions — clean prey sourcing matters most here
</SpeciesOnly>

## Weight management

![Weighing a falcon on a perch scale](/images/nutrition/weighing.png)

In falconry, weight is the steering wheel. A working falcon is kept at its **flying weight** — the weight at which it is keen enough to hunt but heavy enough to stay in good condition. A precise gram scale and a daily weighing routine, recorded on paper or in an app, are essential. Trends matter more than any single reading: a steady drop of more than 5–10% from the bird's normal flying weight without a clear cause is a red flag for illness.

Never weight-manage a sick or moulting bird without veterinary guidance.

## Casting — the daily health check

A few hours after a meal, a healthy falcon **casts** — regurgitates a small dry pellet of indigestible material (fur, feathers, small bone fragments). The casting is one of the single best daily health indicators you have:

- **Healthy:** firm, dry pellet of fur or feather, passed within 12–24 hours.
- **Concerning:** no casting after 24 hours, or wet, foul-smelling casting → suspect sour crop or impaction.

To make sure the bird gets enough roughage, periodically include a small amount of feather (from chicks or game birds) or rabbit fur with the meal. **Do not feed again until the previous casting has been passed.**

## Food preparation and safety

- **Source captive-bred whole prey** from a reputable supplier. Wild-caught food carries disease and toxin risk.
- **Thaw frozen prey slowly in the refrigerator**, never in warm water — warm water encourages the bacterial growth that causes sour crop.
- **Feed within a few hours of thawing.** Discard anything left out at ambient temperature for more than a couple of hours, especially in Gulf summer heat.
- **Keep the feeding area clean.** Wash blocks, perches, and the cadge daily.
- **Inspect every prey item** before feeding — if it smells off or looks discoloured, throw it out. The cost of one quail is nothing compared to a sour-crop emergency.

## Hydration

Falcons get most of their water from prey, but should still have **clean fresh water available daily**, especially in hot weather. As a working reference, an adult sport falcon takes around **50 ml of water per kg of body weight per day**. In Gulf summer, monitor drinking and offer a shallow bath two to three times a week.

## When to call your vet

Any of the following warrants a veterinary call:

- No casting passed after 24 hours
- Refusal to eat for more than 48 hours
- Sour or fluid-filled crop
- Sudden weight loss greater than 10%
- Green or fluorescent-yellow droppings
