A falcon hides illness for as long as it possibly can — wild instinct says weakness invites predation. By the time a sick bird looks obviously sick, the disease is usually well advanced. The single most valuable habit you can build is a fast daily health check that catches small changes early, plus knowing what specific signs point to which problem.

This chapter is organised around three questions:

1. How do I check my falcon today?
2. What is wrong with it when something looks off?
3. How do I keep the next problem from happening?

## The 60-second daily health check

![A falconer examining a saker's foot during the daily check](/images/health/daily-check.png)

Every day, before food, run through this list. It takes a minute and it is the single best diagnostic tool you have.

1. **Stance.** Standing tall and balanced on both feet, or hunched / standing on one foot for long periods? A persistently fluffed-up, hunched posture is a sick-bird posture.
2. **Eyes.** Both bright, fully open, the same. Half-closed, watery, swollen, or asymmetric eyes are a problem.
3. **Nostrils (cere).** Clean and dry. Discharge, crust, swelling, or noisy breathing are respiratory red flags.
4. **Breathing.** Steady, silent, no tail-pumping. Open-mouth breathing at rest, audible clicks or wheezes, or visible tail bobbing with each breath suggest serious respiratory disease.
5. **Crop.** If fed yesterday, the crop should be empty or nearly so. A persistently full or doughy crop after a normal interval is sour crop until proven otherwise.
6. **Casting.** A clean dry pellet should have been passed within 12–24 hours of the last meal with roughage.
7. **Droppings.** Look at colour and consistency (see the next section). Compare to yesterday.
8. **Feet.** Pads smooth, no swelling, no scabs, no heat, no limping. Both feet weight-bearing equally.
9. **Plumage.** Sleek and tidy; no broken feathers, no soiling around the vent.
10. **Weight.** On the scale. Compare to the trend, not just yesterday.

Anything that looks different from the bird's normal — even subtly — gets noted in the log book. Patterns matter: one off morning is rarely an emergency, two in a row is a vet call.

## Reading the droppings

The dropping is the cheapest, most informative diagnostic test you have. A normal falcon dropping has three clearly distinguishable parts: a dark **fecal** portion, a chalky-white **urate** portion, and a small clear **liquid urine** portion.

| What you see | What it usually means |
|---|---|
| Dark brown/black firm fecal + white urate + small liquid | **Normal** for a meat-fed falcon |
| Bright green or yellow urates | Liver problem, or empty gut (recently not eaten) |
| Brown or chocolate urates | Suspect **lead poisoning** (e.g. from shot fragments in wild prey) — vet immediately |
| Pink or red urine/urate | Possible kidney bleeding — vet |
| Watery droppings (much more liquid than usual) | Dehydration, intestinal infection, or stress — observe and rehydrate; vet if persistent |
| Loose green fecal + foul smell | Bacterial/protozoal enteritis; can also indicate aspergillosis | 
| Undigested food in droppings | Gut motility problem — vet |
| No droppings overnight | Anorexia or impaction — investigate |

A single odd dropping is rarely an emergency. A pattern over 24 hours is a vet call.

## Symptom → likely cause

This is a triage tool, not a diagnosis. Use it to know what to suspect and how urgent the vet visit is.

| Sign you observe | First things to suspect | Urgency |
|---|---|---|
| Open-mouth breathing at rest, tail bobbing, exercise intolerance | **Aspergillosis** (top of list, especially for gyrfalcons in Gulf) | **High — call vet today** |
| Yellow / cheesy lesions in the mouth or throat, head shaking, regurgitation | **Frounce (trichomoniasis)** | **High — vet within 24 h** |
| Persistently full crop, sour smell from breath, vomiting | **Sour crop** (crop stasis) | **High — vet today** |
| Swelling, scab, redness, or heat on the underside of the foot; favouring one leg | **Bumblefoot** (pododermatitis) | Moderate — vet within days |
| Pox-like lesions (dry crusts) on cere, eyelids, or unfeathered skin | **Avian pox** | Moderate — vet |
| Sudden death of one bird with little warning, especially after pigeon contact | **Falcon herpesvirus** — quarantine others, vet | **Emergency for the rest of your birds** |
| Sudden weight loss > 10%, lethargy, watery droppings | Many possibilities (parasites, infection, organ disease) | **High — vet** |
| Itching, restless preening, visible parasites in plumage | **Lice or mites** | Low — treat per vet |
| Brown urates, weakness, regurgitation after eating wild-shot prey | **Lead poisoning** | **Emergency — vet immediately** |
| Twisted neck, tremor, imbalance | Advanced infection (often aspergillosis), poisoning, or trauma | **Emergency — vet immediately** |

> This content is for informational purposes only. Diagnosing and treating a falcon requires a veterinarian — these tables narrow down what to suspect and how fast to move, not what to administer.

## The major falcon diseases

### Aspergillosis (fungal pneumonia)

The most serious and most common cause of death in captive falcons in the Gulf. Caused by inhaling *Aspergillus fumigatus* spores from mouldy bedding, damp food, or stagnant environments. The fungus colonises the lungs and air sacs.

**Signs.** Open-mouth breathing at rest, exercise intolerance (the bird tires fast and recovers slowly), weight loss, change in voice, green diarrhoea in advanced cases, eventually neurological signs (twisted neck, tremor).

**Diagnosis.** Stamina test (resting vs post-exercise breathing recovery), endoscopy, blood antibody titres, fungal culture. Done by a vet.

**Why it matters.** Treatment is long and difficult; once neurological signs appear, prognosis is poor. **Prevention is everything**: clean dry mews, no mouldy food, good ventilation, low stress.

<SpeciesOnly species="gyrfalcon">
**Gyrfalcons are the highest-risk species** for aspergillosis in Gulf conditions. If you keep gyrs, run mews ventilation, food storage hygiene, and stress management at the strictest level. Consider prophylactic vet check-ups before and during summer.
</SpeciesOnly>

### Frounce (trichomoniasis)

Caused by the protozoan *Trichomonas gallinae*. Transmitted almost exclusively through eating infected pigeons or doves, or from contaminated water. This is the single best argument for never feeding pigeon.

**Signs.** Yellowish "cheesy" lesions in the mouth, throat, or crop (the diagnostic sign — open the beak gently and look). Difficulty swallowing, head shaking, regurgitation, foul-smelling breath, weight loss, feathers ruffled around the crop in advanced cases.

**Diagnosis.** Visual inspection of the throat plus a wet-mount of crop swab under microscope. Fast for a vet.

**Treatment.** Carnidazole (Spartrix) or metronidazole, prescribed by a vet. Highly treatable if caught early.

<SpeciesOnly species="peregrine">
**Peregrines** are bird specialists in the wild and the species most likely to have been exposed to *Trichomonas* through pigeon prey. If your peregrine was wild-trapped or had any pigeon contact, frounce should be top of the differential list at the first sign of mouth/throat trouble.
</SpeciesOnly>

### Sour crop (crop stasis)

The crop fails to empty. Bacterial and yeast overgrowth follows. The bird becomes systemically toxic within hours to days.

**Signs.** Crop full and doughy long after it should be empty, sour or rancid smell from the beak, regurgitation of foul slimy material, lethargy, refusal to eat. Often a sequel to feeding spoiled or warm-thawed prey, dehydration, or a bird already weakened by another illness.

**Treatment.** **Vet emergency.** Crop is gently flushed with warm saline; underlying cause (infection, stress, bad food) is addressed. Antifungal or antibiotic depending on culture.

**Prevention.** Thaw food in the fridge, never warm water. Don't feed spoiled or fly-touched meat. Don't feed a sick or dehydrated bird before it stabilises.

### Bumblefoot (pododermatitis)

Inflammation and infection of the foot pad. Multifactorial: hard or rough perches, overweight bird, wet or dirty perches, small puncture that gets infected, prolonged unilateral weight bearing because of injury elsewhere.

**Signs.** Smooth shiny patch on the central pad → pink swelling → scab → ulcerated lesion. Bird favours one foot. Heat or swelling visible. Advanced cases: severe lameness, systemic infection.

**Treatment.** Veterinary — local debridement, topical and systemic antibiotic, padded perches, address the cause (corrected weight, soft perch surfaces, cleaner mews).

**Prevention.** Astroturf or padded perches at correct diameter for the species; dry, clean perches; correct flying weight; daily foot inspection.

<SpeciesOnly species="saker">
**Sakers** kept on hard perches in Gulf summer heat are at noticeably higher risk; perch hygiene and padding are the key levers.
</SpeciesOnly>

### Falcon herpesvirus

Severe systemic viral disease, frequently fatal. Transmitted from infected pigeons (a major reservoir) or from contact with infected falcons.

**Signs.** Often little warning — sudden death, sometimes preceded by lethargy, anorexia, and yellow/green liver-pattern droppings for 24–48 h. Post-mortem reveals characteristic liver lesions.

**Treatment.** No specific cure. Supportive care; most cases die. **Prevention is the only real defence**: never feed pigeon, quarantine new birds, hygiene between birds.

### Coccidiosis and other internal parasites

Protozoa (*Caryospora*, *Eimeria*) in the intestinal tract. Common in young captive-bred falcons.

**Signs.** Watery green diarrhoea, weight loss, poor condition despite normal feeding, sometimes blood-tinged droppings.

**Diagnosis.** Faecal flotation/smear by vet — straightforward.

**Treatment.** Toltrazuril or sulphonamide drugs prescribed by vet.

**Prevention.** Faecal screening of new birds; clean mews (faeces source the next infection); avoid overcrowding.

### External parasites (lice, mites, hippoboscid flies)

**Signs.** Restless preening, visible parasites in plumage especially under wings and around vent, damaged feather quality, sometimes anaemia in heavy mite infestations.

**Treatment.** Vet-prescribed permethrin or fipronil sprays applied carefully (raptor-appropriate dose); pyrethrin powders in mild cases. Treat the bird and the mews.

### Lead poisoning

From shot fragments in wild-caught prey or accidental ingestion of lead objects.

**Signs.** Brown-stained urates, weakness, regurgitation, neurological signs, blindness, eventual death.

**Diagnosis.** Blood lead level; X-ray for radiopaque fragments.

**Treatment.** Veterinary chelation therapy — urgent.

**Prevention.** Don't feed wild-shot prey unless you know it was taken with non-lead ammunition.

## Prevention — the daily habits that matter most

![A clean, well-ventilated traditional mews interior](/images/health/clean-mews.png)

In falcon medicine, prevention is the lever, not treatment. Most of the diseases above are far easier to avoid than to cure.

- **Clean, dry, well-ventilated mews.** Daily droppings clear, weekly perch cleaning, monthly deep clean. No mould — mouldy bedding is the single biggest aspergillosis trigger.
- **Strict food hygiene.** Captive-bred whole prey only. Thaw in the fridge. Discard anything questionable. **No pigeon, ever**, unless you control its entire life cycle and quarantine the meat at −20 °C for at least two weeks.
- **Hand hygiene.** Wash hands and disinfect equipment between birds. Don't share gloves between birds without disinfecting.
- **Quarantine new birds for at least 30 days** before mixing with the rest. New bird = unknown disease history. Faecal screen plus a vet check before integration.
- **Daily weight + observation log.** A trend caught at day 2 saves a vet emergency at day 7.
- **Regular vet check-ups.** At minimum: pre-season fitness check, mid-moult check, end-of-season check. Many Gulf falcon hospitals offer routine packages.
- **Vaccination where available.** Avian pox vaccination is available in some regions; ask your vet.
- **Manage stress.** Stressed birds are immune-suppressed and more prone to everything above. Calm handling, consistent routine, appropriate housing.

## A basic falconry first-aid kit

![A falconry first-aid kit, flat lay](/images/health/first-aid.png)

Keep these on hand. None of them replace a vet, but they let you stabilise a bird while you reach one.

- A digital gram scale you trust
- A pen torch (for examining mouth, throat, eyes)
- Sterile saline (for flushing wounds, eyes, or crop under vet guidance)
- Cotton swabs and gauze
- Vet wrap (cohesive bandage)
- Styptic powder or ferric chloride pencil (for broken blood feather or talon bleed)
- Hand-warming gloves and a small towel (for restraining safely)
- Rehydration solution (oral electrolyte for raptors — vet-supplied)
- Thermometer (cloacal, for serious cases under vet guidance)
- Phone numbers of two raptor vets, written down, not just on the phone

## When to call the vet vs. monitor at home

**Call immediately:**
- Open-mouth breathing at rest, tail-pumping breathing
- Visible mouth/throat lesions
- Persistently full crop, sour breath, vomiting
- Sudden severe weight loss, sudden refusal to eat
- Brown urates, blood in droppings, neurological signs (twisting, tremor)
- Severe trauma, broken blood feather that won't stop bleeding

**Monitor closely (vet within days):**
- Mild change in droppings persisting > 24 h
- Slight weight drift outside normal range
- Mild foot redness or scab
- Mild plumage parasites
- Quietness without other signs

**Log it but don't panic:**
- One off dropping
- One refused meal in an otherwise well bird
- A single broken non-blood feather

The reflex you want to build is **observe → log → compare to trend → act**. Most Gulf raptor vets would rather you call once unnecessarily than miss an emergency.

## Tendencies by species

The diseases and diagnostic tools above apply to all three falcons. What changes between them is **risk profile, sensitivity, and what you watch for first**. Switch tabs above to see the species you keep.

<SpeciesOnly species="saker">
### Saker tendencies

The saker is the **default Gulf falcon for good reason**. Native to Central Asia and well adapted to hot, arid climates — the most heat-tolerant of the three primary falcons and the most forgiving of small husbandry mistakes.

**What you watch for first**
- **Bumblefoot** — sakers spend long hours on perches in Gulf summer; a heavier saker on a hot hard perch is the textbook setup. Padded perches, daily foot check, and weight discipline are the levers.
- **Rodent-borne issues** — because sakers tolerate occasional rat or mouse in the diet, they have slightly more exposure to internal parasites than peregrines. Routine faecal screening once or twice a year covers this.
- **Generally hardier** — the saker's broader appetite and constitution mean a small mistake (one off meal, brief heat spike) is less likely to escalate. This is *not* a licence to be careless; it's a reason the saker is the Gulf workhorse.

**Good-condition signals**: alert posture, smooth dry foot pads, firm dropping with clear three-part split, steady flying weight that holds week to week.
</SpeciesOnly>

<SpeciesOnly species="peregrine">
### Peregrine tendencies

The peregrine is the **most physiologically sensitive** of the three. Smaller bird, smaller margin, more reactive nervous system. Husbandry that works fine for a saker can be too much for a peregrine.

**What you watch for first**
- **Frounce (trichomoniasis)** — the peregrine is a bird specialist by nature; if it has *any* contact with pigeon — yours, your neighbour's, even prey raised near pigeons — frounce is the first thing to check at any sign of mouth or throat trouble.
- **Handling stress** — peregrines crash from over-handling alone. Calmer manning routine, fewer disturbances, no unnecessary visitors during early training. A stressed peregrine is an immune-suppressed peregrine.
- **Sour crop progresses faster** — proportionally smaller crop means less buffer. A peregrine with a slow-emptying crop becomes a vet emergency hours sooner than a saker would.
- **Weight discipline matters more** — smaller absolute body weight means a 50 g swing matters more than the same 50 g on a saker. Daily weighing, consistent flying weight, smaller more frequent meals on heavy training days.

**Good-condition signals**: sleek tight feathers, calm but alert posture, quiet steady breathing, narrow stable weight band over weeks.
</SpeciesOnly>

<SpeciesOnly species="gyrfalcon">
### Gyrfalcon tendencies

The gyrfalcon is the **highest-maintenance and highest-risk** of the three in Gulf conditions. Cold-climate origin (Arctic and sub-Arctic) means everything about Gulf summer works against this species. Most rewarding falcon for an experienced keeper, least forgiving for a beginner.

**What you watch for first**
- **Aspergillosis** — the single biggest killer of captive gyrfalcons in the Gulf. Once symptoms appear, treatment success is measurably lower in gyrs than in sakers or peregrines. **Prevention is the entire game**: dry well-ventilated mews, no mouldy bedding or food, low stress, prophylactic vet check before and during summer.
- **Heat stress** — gyrs show open-mouth panting and gular flutter at ambient temperatures where sakers are still comfortable. Reduced appetite in summer is a signal, not a quirk. Cooler shaded mews, more frequent bathing (every 2–3 days), avoid handling during the hottest hours.
- **Lower exercise tolerance in heat** — hunting and training in Gulf summer should shift to early morning or evening; midday work is risky.
- **Stricter food hygiene than other species** — because aspergillosis risk is so high, food storage, thawing, and feeding-area cleanliness matter more for gyrs than for any other falcon. One mouldy meal can start a chain that ends in death weeks later.

**Good-condition signals**: white firm dropping with full urate cap, steady appetite even in summer, normal resting respiration, no panting at moderate temperatures, consistent flying weight without seasonal collapse.
</SpeciesOnly>
