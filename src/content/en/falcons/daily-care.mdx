Daily care is the work. Nutrition tells you *what* to feed, Health tells you *what to watch for*, Supplements tell you *what to add when* — but **the day-by-day routine is what holds it all together**. A falcon that is well-fed, well-housed, and given the right vitamins but handled inconsistently will still decline. The rhythm is the foundation; everything else is built on it.

This chapter covers the actual hands-on flow of a working falcon's day, the new vocabulary you need (manning, weathering, hooding, jesses, the cadge), and the seasonal rhythms across the year.

## The daily routine

Same time, same order, every day. The bird learns the pattern and stays calm. The pattern matters more than its specific contents — a falcon kept on a consistent rough routine is healthier than one kept on a perfect routine that changes weekly.

A working morning typically runs:

1. **Weight on the scale** — same scale, same time, before food. (See Nutrition for flying weight; see Health for the 5–10% drop rule.)
2. **The 60-second health check** — stance, eyes, cere, breathing, crop, casting, droppings, feet, plumage. (Full checklist in Health.)
3. **Feed or fly first** — depending on training plan. Hunting day birds are often flown first then fed; rest day birds are simply fed.
4. **Equipment check** — jesses, leash, swivel, hood, glove all inspected for wear.
5. **Tidy the daily room (غرفة الطيور)** — droppings cleared, perches wiped, water freshened.

The evening flow is shorter: a calm observation, a brief weight check if you're in a tight training cycle, then secure the bird for the night.

## Manning

Manning is the calm, repeated, low-stress acclimation of the bird to your hand, your glove, your voice, and your daily presence. It is the **single most important skill of the early weeks** with any new falcon. A well-manned bird is easier to train, easier to medicate, easier to handle in an emergency. A poorly-manned bird fights you forever.

The principles:

- **Trust is built over weeks, not days.** A two-hour intensive session is worse than fifteen minutes a day for a fortnight.
- **Quiet environment first.** Low light, minimal movement around you, no other people, no other animals.
- **No direct eye contact in the early days.** Predator behaviour. Look past the bird, not at it.
- **Smooth predictable movements.** No sudden gestures. Speak softly and consistently.
- **Gradual exposure** to normal household sound, then daylight, then strangers, in that order.
- **Stop on a good note.** End each session before the bird is stressed, not after.

A bird that has been properly manned will stand calmly on the glove during a vet exam without a hood. That is the standard.

## Handling

The mechanics of picking the bird up, putting it down, and securing it on the glove. Three things that matter every time:

- **Jess control.** When the bird is on your glove, the jesses are held between your gloved fingers. You control where the bird can go before the bird does. This protects the bird and you.
- **Hooding.** Sliding the leather hood smoothly over the head from in front, never from behind. The hood transitions the bird from "alert" to "rested" — a hooded falcon is calm because it's in dignified darkness, not because it's been overpowered.
- **Smooth, predictable, unhurried.** Every transfer should look like a single continuous motion. Never grab. Never rush.

The cadge — a portable padded perch carried on long journeys or used to hold multiple birds — uses the same principles, scaled up.

## Weighing

![A saker falcon on a perch scale during the daily weigh-in](/images/nutrition/weighing.png)

The daily weight is the steering wheel of the falconer-falcon partnership. It tells you whether your bird is fit, whether it's keen, and — most importantly — whether something is wrong before any other sign appears.

Rules of the weighing routine:

- **Same scale.** Different scales drift. Pick one and use it.
- **Same time.** First thing in the morning, before food, after the bird has cast. Anything else introduces noise.
- **Same conditions.** Bird unhooded or hooded the same way; perched on the scale calmly; no fluttering.
- **Logged.** A logbook or app — the **trend** over weeks matters more than any single number.
- **Trends not totals.** A consistent slow drift below the bird's normal flying weight is a much louder signal than a single high reading.

A drop of more than **5–10% from the bird's normal flying weight** without a clear reason is a red flag for illness. (See Health for the symptom triage that follows.)

## Feeding routine

What to feed lives in the Nutrition chapter. **How the meal happens** is daily-care material:

- **Same place every day.** A consistent feeding location reduces stress and reinforces routine.
- **Calm, never rushed.** A stressed bird at the food is a bird that bolts food, then casts poorly, then becomes susceptible to sour crop.
- **Inspect every prey item before feeding.** A glance, a sniff. If something smells off, throw it out.
- **Present on the glove or in a clean tray.** Never on the floor of the daily room.
- **Remove uneaten leftovers within the hour.** Especially in summer heat. Spoiled meat left in the room is a sour-crop emergency waiting to happen.
- **Watch the casting clock.** No second meal until the previous casting has been passed (typically 12–24 hours).

## Weathering

![A saker calmly perched on a traditional outdoor block at golden hour](/images/daily-care/weathering.png)

Weathering is **outdoor time on a block or bow perch** — sun, fresh air, exercise, mental stimulation, and natural temperature regulation. A falcon that lives only indoors loses condition. A falcon that weathers daily stays sleek, alert, and even-tempered.

The basics:

- **A safe, secure perch** in a quiet spot — protected from dogs, children, and other birds.
- **Tethered safely** with a swivel and an appropriately-rated leash. Check the knot every time.
- **Within sight or earshot** — never leave a tethered bird out of supervision range.
- **Shade available** so the bird can self-regulate temperature.
- **Fresh shallow bath water** within reach two to three times a week.

**In Gulf summer**, the rule changes. Weathering shifts to **early morning and late afternoon** — never midday. Open shade only. The block itself can become hot enough to cause foot damage; check it with the back of your hand before placing the bird. (See the Health chapter for bumblefoot prevention; this is one of its main causes.)

## Daily hygiene of the daily room (غرفة الطيور)

Five minutes a day prevents most of the housing-related diseases in the Health chapter:

- **Droppings cleared** from under perches and around the floor
- **Perches wiped** — Astroturf or padded covers checked for damp or droppings build-up
- **Water bowl refreshed** with clean water
- **Ventilation checked** — air moving, no stagnant pockets
- **Floor swept** of feathers, casting fragments, and any spilled food

Once a week: a deeper clean of perches and floor surfaces. Once a month: full disinfection. Damp, mouldy bedding is the single biggest aspergillosis trigger; the daily room must be kept dry.

## Equipment care

![Traditional Arabian falconry equipment laid out — hood, jesses, swivel, glove](/images/daily-care/equipment.png)

The kit you live with daily:

- **Hood** — a well-fitted leather hood. Daily check for cracks, loose stitching, or hardened leather. Conditioned periodically with a small amount of leather oil.
- **Jesses** — soft leather straps on the bird's legs. **Daily inspection for fraying** — a chafed jess can rub the tarsus raw. Replace at the first sign of significant wear.
- **Swivel** — small brass or steel swivel between the jesses and the leash. Keeps the leash from twisting. Check it spins freely; replace if seized.
- **Leash** — braided or flat, long enough for the bird to move on the perch but not reach the ground or another bird. **Inspect daily for fraying**; this is the single most important piece of equipment for not losing a bird.
- **Glove** — heavy leather, long enough to protect the wrist and forearm. Conditioned periodically. Cleaned of blood and food after every session.
- **Cadge** — the portable padded perch for transport or holding multiple birds. Keep covers clean.

The principle is simple: **bad equipment kills birds**. A broken leash and the bird is gone. A chafed jess and the leg is sore for weeks. Five minutes of daily inspection saves emergencies.

## Seasonal adjustments

The daily routine isn't static across the year — it shifts with the season and with what the bird is doing.

- **Hunting season** — daily training, tightest weight management, frequent handling, full equipment use. The most demanding routine.
- **Off-season / rest** — lighter handling, lower-intensity routine, full rations, less frequent weighing. The bird recovers condition.
- **Pre-moult transition** — preparing the bird and its housing for the moult; cleaning, replacing worn equipment, transitioning to summer feed and supplement schedule.
- **Moult / المقيض** — in traditional Gulf practice, the bird transitions to a dedicated **summer moulting chamber, the المقيض** — a darkened, climate-controlled space where it is largely undisturbed for several weeks while it sheds and regrows feathers. Daily handling drops to a minimum; food is delivered without ceremony; weight management is relaxed. *Detailed treatment of the المقيض itself — its design, climate control, and the moult management protocol inside it — comes in the Housing & Environment chapter.*

## Building the rhythm

The daily routine isn't paperwork. It is what turns a bird into *your* bird. Consistency, patience, and the long arc of the partnership matter more than any single technique. The falcon learns your rhythm; you learn the falcon's. Over months you stop noticing what's normal — and that's exactly when you'll spot what's not.

## Tendencies by species

The routine above applies to all three falcons. What differs is **how strict the consistency must be, and where the routine bends with the season**. Switch the species tabs at the top to see your bird.

<SpeciesOnly species="saker">
### Saker — daily care notes

The saker is the **most forgiving of routine breaks** of the three. A skipped weighing, a late feed, a slightly different handling environment — the saker absorbs these without breaking down. This is why the saker is the working Gulf falcon: the routine that survives real life suits it.

**Where the routine still matters:**
- **Daily inspection** is non-negotiable, even with a forgiving species. The saker hides illness as well as any falcon.
- **Bumblefoot watch** in summer means perch checks are part of every weathering session — see Health for the full bumblefoot story.
- **Generally robust handling** — the saker tolerates new people and new places more readily than a peregrine.

A well-routined saker is calm, predictable, and almost self-managing. Most days the routine takes the time it takes; rare days it gets compressed; the bird stays steady.
</SpeciesOnly>

<SpeciesOnly species="peregrine">
### Peregrine — daily care notes

The peregrine **punishes inconsistency**. Same time, same order, same place every day matters more for this species than for either of the other two. A peregrine kept on a slightly variable routine never settles fully; a peregrine kept on a strict routine becomes one of the steadiest, most precise birds in falconry.

**Where the routine matters most:**
- **Quiet, predictable handling** every time. Avoid surprise visitors, avoid loud handling environments, avoid sudden routine changes.
- **Manning takes longer** — invest the time in the early weeks; the payoff lasts the bird's working life.
- **Weighing precision matters more** — the peregrine's smaller body means smaller absolute weight swings carry the same diagnostic weight as larger swings on a saker.
- **Feeding routine must be calm** — a stressed peregrine at food is a peregrine that bolts and casts poorly.

This is the species where the rhythm visibly *is* the partnership.
</SpeciesOnly>

<SpeciesOnly species="gyrfalcon">
### Gyrfalcon — daily care notes

The gyrfalcon's daily care is **dominated by the seasonal calendar** more than any other falcon. Cold-climate origin means Gulf summer fundamentally reshapes what daily care looks like for half the year.

**Where the routine bends most heavily:**
- **Weathering hours** — early morning and late afternoon only in summer. Midday weathering is not "watch carefully", it's "do not". The gyrfalcon does not tolerate Gulf midday heat.
- **Mews ventilation** — the daily check matters more for this species (aspergillosis risk; see Health and Supplements).
- **Bathing more frequently** — every 2–3 days in summer rather than weekly.
- **Handling during the hottest hours minimised** — even brief restraint adds heat load.
- **Pre-moult / المقيض transition** is the centrepiece of the year — preparing the moulting chamber and the bird for the summer is a major project for any serious gyrfalcon keeper.

Outside of summer, the gyr's daily care looks much like a saker's. In summer, every part of the routine is conditioned by heat.
</SpeciesOnly>
