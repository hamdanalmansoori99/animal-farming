Breeds and types matter more than most new falconers realise. Performance, climate tolerance, temperament, and even illness susceptibility vary across subspecies and colour morphs of the same species — a white-morph gyrfalcon and a grey-morph gyrfalcon are biologically the same bird but can feel like different species on the glove. A tundra peregrine and a mediterranean peregrine bring different strengths. Knowing what you have, and what to look for in what you're being offered, shapes every other decision downstream.

This chapter covers the three species kept on the farm — saker, peregrine, gyrfalcon — their subspecies and colour morphs, the common Gulf-bred hybrids, gender differences, age and origin terminology, and a practical closing section on choosing a bird. For what to **do** with the bird once you have it, see **Nutrition**, **Health & Prevention**, **Housing & Environment**, **Daily Care**, and **Training**.

## Saker — *Falco cherrug*

The working Gulf falcon. Adult sakers weigh roughly **730–1300 grams** — females larger, as with all three species — and historically ranged from central and eastern Europe across the Central Asian steppes. In the wild they are **generalist hunters**, taking ground mammals (sousliks, gerbils) as readily as birds (sandgrouse, larks). That generalist diet is exactly why the saker is so forgiving to keep: the species evolved to adjust.

### Recognised subspecies

- ***F. c. cherrug*** — the **nominate** subspecies, the "steppe saker", ranging from central Europe through Kazakhstan
- ***F. c. milvipes*** — the "mountain saker", widespread across most of Central Asia; the subspecies the Altai morph belongs to (see below)
- ***F. c. coatsi*** — the **desert saker** of Kazakhstan and Uzbekistan
- ***F. c. hendersoni*** — the **high-elevation saker** of Tibet and the Qinghai Plateau

Captive-bred sakers in the Gulf market today are often a mix of *cherrug* and *milvipes* lineage, with a few operations specialising in particular regional types.

### The Altai falcon — not a separate species

For decades the **Altai falcon** — a darker, more heavily spotted form found at the northeast edge of the saker range — was argued about. Some taxonomists treated it as a distinct species, *Falco altaicus*; others suspected it was a natural saker × gyrfalcon hybrid. A 2023 genetic study by Zinevich and colleagues, published in *Scientific Reports*, settled the question: the Altai falcon is a **colour morph within the Central Asian *F. c. milvipes***, not a separate taxon and not a hybrid. It shares a population with nearby milvipes sakers and is not genetically distinct.

In practical terms: an Altai-morph saker is a saker. It is prized for its colour, but biology, husbandry, training, and veterinary care are identical to any other milvipes saker.

### Gulf naming

The saker is **الحرّ** (*al-hurr*). Regional Gulf vocabulary includes age and colour-phase names — red saker, black saker, and several juvenile-year descriptors — but these are **market vocabulary**, not scientific taxonomy, and can vary between emirates and tribes. The subspecies label matters for provenance; the market label matters for price.

### What to look for when choosing a saker

- **Plumage completeness** — full tail, full wings, no broken or missing primaries or secondaries
- **Feet and legs** — no swelling, no scars that suggest old bumblefoot, no asymmetry
- **Eyes** — clear, both pupils responding equally, no discharge
- **Keel** — palpate gently: a sharp keel suggests the bird is underweight, a well-muscled keel is desirable
- **Provenance documentation** — captive-bred certificate, microchip, breeding pair identification where possible

## Peregrine — *Falco peregrinus*

The speed specialist. Adult peregrines weigh roughly **600–1100 grams** — smaller and more compact than sakers. The peregrine holds the record as the fastest animal on Earth in a stoop, with measured dives over 320 km/h. In the wild it is a **bird specialist**, taking ducks, pigeons, and smaller passerines in fast aerial attacks.

Peregrines have an enormous global range — nearly every continent — and ornithologists currently recognise **about 19 subspecies**. Coverage of all 19 is out of scope for this chapter; the ones most relevant to a Gulf-falconry audience are below.

### Six subspecies worth knowing

- ***F. p. peregrinus*** — the **nominate** Eurasian peregrine, from Europe through central Asia
- ***F. p. anatum*** — the **American "duck hawk"**, Rocky Mountains and beyond; historically the subspecies of United States falconry
- ***F. p. calidus*** — the **tundra peregrine**, breeding in high-Arctic Russia and Scandinavia, **wintering in the Middle East, East Africa, and South Asia**. This is most likely the peregrine Gulf falconers historically encountered in passage.
- ***F. p. babylonicus*** — the **red-naped shaheen**, from eastern Iran through the Hindu Kush and Tian Shan; a smaller, more saker-like peregrine
- ***F. p. peregrinator*** — the **black shaheen**, resident across South Asia from the Indian subcontinent to Sri Lanka
- ***F. p. brookei*** — the **Mediterranean peregrine**, sometimes called the "Maltese falcon", from southern France and Spain through the Mediterranean basin

### The "shaheen" question

**الشاهين** — *al-shaheen* — is one of the oldest names in Arabic falconry, and exactly which subspecies it refers to depends on who you ask and where. In Gulf tradition the word is most often associated with **calidus**, the tundra peregrine that winters in the region and was historically available in passage. But **babylonicus** (red-naped) and **peregrinator** (black) both carry the "shaheen" name in parts of the wider Arabic-speaking and South Asian world. The word predates scientific taxonomy and doesn't map cleanly onto it. Modern captive-bred peregrines in the Gulf market are often of mixed subspecies lineage, so the question becomes academic for most working birds.

### What to look for when choosing a peregrine

- **Barring clarity** — the dark bars across the lower belly should be clean and regular; uneven or mottled barring can indicate developmental stress
- **Cere colour** — yellow in adults, blue-grey in juveniles; worth confirming against the stated age
- **Eyes** — as for saker, both clear and responsive
- **Legs and feet** — peregrine feet are proportionally smaller and more delicate than saker feet; check for integrity carefully
- **Provenance** — captive-bred documentation, and **ask about the parent subspecies** if subspecies matters to you

## Gyrfalcon — *Falco rusticolus*

The largest falcon in the world, and the most demanding to keep in Gulf conditions. Adult gyrs weigh **800–2100 grams** with the heaviest females far exceeding any saker. The gyrfalcon's wild range is **circumpolar Arctic and sub-Arctic** — Greenland, Iceland, northern Scandinavia, Siberia, Alaska, northern Canada. In the wild it is a bird specialist, hunting ptarmigan and ducks across vast open tundra.

The gyrfalcon is **monotypic** — no recognised subspecies — but shows dramatic colour variation along a **continuous cline** that plays a major role in Gulf-market pricing.

### Colour morphs

- **White morph** — concentrated in Greenland, nearly pure white with light markings on the wings and tail. This is the most prized morph culturally and commercially, and in the Gulf market white gyrs command substantial premiums.
- **Silver morph** — pale with a soft greyish cast; at a distance can resemble an oversized lanner falcon
- **Grey morph** — the most common globally, intermediate tones across the back and wings
- **Dark (black) morph** — dark grey to almost black in some individuals; more common in northern Canada

Many individual gyrfalcons don't cleanly fit any single category. The morphs are not discrete — they sit on a continuous cline, and a "silver-grey" or "grey-dark" bird is perfectly ordinary.

### Gulf naming

The gyrfalcon is **الجير** (*al-jeer*). White-morph gyrfalcons are sometimes given specific regional names reflecting their status.

### What matters most with a gyrfalcon

- **Provenance and documentation** — gyrfalcons are **CITES-listed** (Appendix I/II depending on origin); international trade is tightly regulated, and captive-bred certification with full paperwork is non-negotiable for any legal acquisition
- **Plumage condition** — especially for white morphs, where broken or dirty feathers are more visible and more consequential
- **Eye clarity** — as for the other species
- **Respiratory soundness** — gyrs are the species most vulnerable to aspergillosis (see **Health & Prevention**), so any sign of laboured breathing, head-shaking, or discharge during inspection is a hard no

## Hybrids

Hybrid falcons — deliberate crosses between two species — are common in Gulf falconry breeding. They don't belong to any one species page, which is why this section sits outside the tabs above.

### Gyr × Saker

**The flagship Gulf hybrid.** Combines the size and raw power of the gyrfalcon with the heat tolerance and temperament of the saker. The result is typically a falcon larger than a typical saker, steadier than a typical gyrfalcon in Gulf heat, and capable of taking heavier quarry than either parent could manage individually in the same conditions. Gyr × saker hybrids are the backbone of serious working falconry in the UAE, Qatar, and Saudi Arabia.

### Gyr × Peregrine

Rarer, dramatic, stoop-oriented. Inherits some of the peregrine's aerial speed with some of the gyrfalcon's power. Also inherits **some of the gyrfalcon's heat sensitivity** — not as heat-tolerant as a gyr × saker cross. Best suited to cooler-season work.

### Saker × Peregrine

Less common in Gulf tradition than the two gyr hybrids above. Intermediate in size and behaviour; sometimes called a "perlin" or "sakeret" informally. Useful for specific purposes but not a typical flagship bird.

### What to expect from any hybrid

Hybrid vigour is a real phenomenon but not a guarantee — individual variation within a single hybrid clutch can be wide. Two siblings from the same gyr × saker pair may grow into noticeably different birds. **Documentation of the breeding cross matters**: the parent species, the age and lineage of each parent, the exact date of the cross, and any previous offspring from the pair.

## Gender — female versus tiercel

Across all three species, the female is the larger bird — roughly **30% heavier than the male** — and in falconry she is the one usually referred to simply as "the falcon". The male is called the **tiercel** (from Latin *tertius*, "a third", because he is approximately a third smaller).

- **Females** are preferred in Gulf falconry for power, size, and the range of quarry they can take. The vast majority of working falcons in the Gulf are female.
- **Tiercels** (males) are more agile, often faster in a stoop, and take smaller quarry efficiently. They are preferred for some specialised purposes — small game, lure work, certain aerial disciplines.

Arabic falconry terminology for males and females exists and varies by region. If the reader has specific preferred regional terminology, we'll incorporate it here during revision.

## Age and origin — falconry terminology

The age at which a falcon is taken or raised affects its behaviour, training trajectory, and legal status. Key terms:

- **Eyass** — a bird taken from the nest **before fledging** and hand-raised. Imprints on the falconer; becomes very tame but is also prone to **screaming** (learned attention-seeking vocalisation) and other imprint-related behavioural quirks. Most modern eyasses are captive-bred rather than wild-collected.
- **Brancher** — a bird taken **just after fledging**, when young are still near the nest on nearby branches. Intermediate between eyass and passage — partly wild in instinct, partly human-accepting.
- **Passage** — a **juvenile caught during its first autumn migration**. Has developed wild hunting instincts but hasn't yet learned to avoid humans. Historically the traditional working bird of falconry; now legal only in specific jurisdictions and seasons.
- **Haggard** — an **adult wild-caught bird** with a year or more of wild experience. Historically prized for hunting ability but **illegal in most jurisdictions today**.
- **Captive-bred** — the modern ethical and legal standard worldwide. Hatched in a registered breeding facility from known parents, with full documentation. **The overwhelming majority of modern falconry birds are captive-bred**, and Gulf breeding operations produce many thousands annually.

## Choosing a falcon — practical closing

The preceding sections cover what the species are. This section covers how to **choose**.

### Questions to ask a breeder

- Who are the parents? Ages, lineage, breeding history with this pair
- When did the bird hatch? Verify against its current plumage and development
- Health record — any medical interventions, vaccinations, known issues
- Moult history if the bird has already moulted
- Casting and weight records if available
- Reason for sale — relevant for older birds

### Documentation that should come with the bird

- **CITES certification** for any international trade, regardless of species — required by law in most jurisdictions
- **Microchip** — inserted under the skin; scannable by any vet
- **Veterinary certificate** from a licensed avian vet within the last few weeks
- **Captive-bred certificate** from the breeding operation

### Physical inspection checklist

Feet, eyes, cere, plumage, keel. The detailed notes for each species above apply. Spend at least 15 unhurried minutes with the bird on the glove before committing, and if possible examine it in both natural and indoor light.

### Red flags — walk away if you see

- Untraceable provenance or missing documentation
- Visible injury — broken feathers, swellings, discharge, lameness
- Poor body condition — sharp keel, prominent hip bones, dull eye
- Seller discomfort with close physical examination
- Pressure to commit quickly without a vet check
- Any sign of respiratory distress — especially in gyrfalcons

### Where to source

- National and international falconry associations — IAF, NAFA, UK Hawk Board, regional Gulf associations
- Registered, licensed captive breeders with documented operations
- Gulf falcon markets — verify provenance and documentation carefully before buying
- Reputable Gulf falcon hospitals — they often offer **pre-purchase examination services** and may know which breeders they consider reliable

## Tendencies by species

The subspecies, morphs, and hybrids above apply to all three species. What differs is **which ones you're most likely to encounter in Gulf falconry, and what the name typically means when a falconer uses it.** Switch the tabs above to see your species.

### Saker — what you'll actually see

In Gulf practice, most working sakers in captive-bred market today are a mix of **milvipes** (mountain) and **cherrug** (steppe) lineage, with a minority of specialised lines tracing to specific regional types. The **Altai morph** is prized for its colour but biologically identical to other milvipes sakers — treat it the same for nutrition, health, and training. **Females** are the overwhelming working standard; a tiercel saker is uncommon at the tier of serious falconry work.

The saker is the species for which market vocabulary (red, black, chocolate) and scientific taxonomy diverge most. Don't confuse colour-phase market labels with subspecies identity.
</SpeciesOnly>

### Peregrine — what you'll actually see

In Gulf practice, the **shaheen** name is most often applied to **calidus**-lineage peregrines that historically wintered in the region, but modern captive-bred peregrines may be of **mixed-subspecies lineage** from international breeding programmes, and the subspecies question becomes academic for most working birds. What matters more than the exact subspecies: **provenance, captive-bred certification, and the physical inspection checklist above**.

**Peregrinator** (black shaheen) and **babylonicus** (red-naped shaheen) occasionally appear in specialised breeding programmes; they're uncommon compared to the more widely-bred temperate-zone peregrines. Females are the standard for serious work, as with saker.
</SpeciesOnly>

<SpeciesOnly species="gyrfalcon">
### Gyrfalcon — what you'll actually see

In Gulf practice, the **colour-morph discussion is where ownership actually lives**. White-morph gyrfalcons command substantial premiums in Gulf markets and are the centrepiece of serious gyrfalcon keeping; grey-morph gyrs are far more commonly available and no less capable biologically — you pay the premium for the white morph for cultural and aesthetic reasons, not for hunting performance.

The **CITES and provenance question matters most for gyrs** of any species covered in this chapter — international trade is heavily regulated, and any gyrfalcon offered without complete documentation should be treated with extreme scepticism. Heat sensitivity and aspergillosis risk are inseparable from gyrfalcon keeping in the Gulf; see **Housing & Environment** and **Health & Prevention** for the full picture.
</SpeciesOnly>
